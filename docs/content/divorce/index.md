# How to get divorced in England and Wales without a solicitor — the no-fault divorce process from start to finish

Getting divorced in England and Wales is now a largely administrative process. Since April 2022, when the Divorce, Dissolution and Separation Act 2020 came into force, you no longer need to blame your spouse or prove that the marriage has broken down through their fault. You simply state that the marriage has broken down irretrievably. The court accepts that statement. There is no argument about whether the divorce can proceed on its merits — though the process can still become complicated in other ways, including disputes about jurisdiction or difficulties with service.

This guide explains the process from beginning to end: what you file, when you file it, what happens in between, and what the divorce itself does and does not resolve. It is written for people going through this without a solicitor. It will not tell you what decisions to make. It will make sure you understand exactly what is happening at each stage before you are standing in the middle of it.

---

## Before you start: what divorce does and does not deal with

This is the single most important thing to understand before you file anything.

A divorce ends your marriage. It does not sort out your finances, and it does not make decisions about your children. Those are separate legal processes — financial remedy and child arrangements — that run alongside the divorce on their own tracks. The divorce itself has no power to divide your property, close off pension claims, or determine where your children live.

Many people discover this too late. They assume that once the divorce is done, everything else follows. It does not. You can be legally divorced and still have an unresolved financial claim your ex can make against you years later — unless a separate financial order is in place.

If you have finances to resolve — property, pensions, savings, income — you need to start the financial remedy process separately, and in most cases it makes sense to start it while the divorce is still in progress, not after. Read the financial remedy guide for a full explanation of how that process works.

## The divorce process: an overview

The no-fault divorce process has five main stages.

First, one or both of you files an application stating that the marriage has broken down irretrievably. Second, if only one of you filed, the other is served with the application and has the opportunity to acknowledge it. Third, after a 20-week cooling-off period from the date the application was issued, the applicant can apply for a conditional order — the court's confirmation that there is no reason why you cannot divorce. Fourth, after a further six weeks and one day from the conditional order, either party can apply for the final order, which legally ends the marriage. The fifth element is not a stage in the process itself but runs alongside it: making sure your finances and, if relevant, arrangements for your children are resolved before or around the same time as the final order.

The minimum time from filing to final order is around 26 weeks — six months — assuming everything moves without delay. In practice, it often takes longer.

## Who can file, and where

To divorce in England and Wales, at least one of you must be domiciled in England or Wales, or must have been habitually resident here for at least 12 months immediately before the application. The marriage must have lasted at least one year before you can apply.

You file online through MyHMCTS, the court's digital case management system. You will need to create an account if you do not already have one. The application is made on a form called D8. You can apply as a sole applicant — meaning you file alone and your spouse is served — or as joint applicants, where you both apply together from the start.

**Joint applications** mean both parties are joint applicants from the outset. Decisions about progressing to the conditional order and final order require both parties to act.

**Sole applications** mean one party files and the other is served. The person who files is the applicant. The person served is the respondent. The applicant controls the timing of applying for the conditional order and final order, subject to the waiting periods described below.

Which route is right for you depends on your circumstances. Joint applications work well where both parties agree the marriage is over and want to cooperate. A sole application may be more appropriate where cooperation is difficult or where one party wants control over the timing.

## Stage 1: Filing the application

The application is filed on Form D8 through MyHMCTS. You will need basic information about your marriage: when and where you married, your full names and addresses, and whether there are any children of the family under 18 or over 18 and in education or training.

You will also need your marriage certificate. If you do not have the original, you can order a certified copy from the General Register Office before you file.

When you file the application, you pay a court fee. **Check GOV.UK for the current figure before you apply** — court fees change periodically. If you are on a low income, you may be eligible for fee remission, which reduces or waives the fee entirely. The application for fee remission is made through the same online system.

Once filed, the court issues the application. The date of issue is important: it starts the 20-week clock that runs to the earliest date you can apply for the conditional order.

---

## Stage 2: Service and acknowledgement

If you are a sole applicant, the court serves the application on your spouse — sends them the paperwork. This is usually done by post to the address you provided. Your spouse then has 14 days to complete and return an Acknowledgement of Service, confirming they have received the application.

If your spouse acknowledges service and does not indicate any intention to dispute the application, the process moves forward. Under no-fault divorce, a spouse cannot contest the divorce itself on the grounds that the marriage has not broken down — the sole ground for divorce is irretrievable breakdown, and the statement that the marriage has broken down irretrievably is not open to challenge. What they can contest is jurisdiction — whether England and Wales is the right country to deal with the divorce — or procedural matters. Contesting the divorce itself on its merits is no longer possible under the 2020 Act.

If your spouse does not respond to service — does not return the Acknowledgement of Service — there are several procedural routes available to you depending on the circumstances. These can include applications for deemed service, alternative service, or in some cases dispensation of service altogether. The appropriate route depends on the specific situation, and the court will give directions on how to proceed.

If your spouse's address is unknown, there are provisions for service by an alternative method. This is one of the less common situations where additional legal assistance is often sought.

---

## Stage 3: The 20-week cooling-off period

After the application is issued, there is a mandatory 20-week waiting period before you can apply for the conditional order. This period is built into the process by the 2020 Act. It is not something that can be shortened by either party or by the court in most circumstances.

The purpose of the waiting period is to give both parties time to reflect, and to allow financial and child arrangements matters to progress alongside the divorce rather than being rushed to a conclusion. It is not a period in which the court expects anything specific from you, unless there are other proceedings running in parallel.

This is the period when, if you have not already done so, you should be actively working on any financial matters. The financial remedy process has its own timeline and its own demands — it does not wait for the divorce to finish.

Use this period to get financial disclosure in order, to understand what a financial settlement might look like, and to take stock of what arrangements exist for any children. It is also a period in which some couples do reconsider. In many cases an application can be withdrawn, although the procedural position depends on the stage the proceedings have reached.

---

## Stage 4: The conditional order

Once the 20-week period has passed and you are satisfied you want to proceed, the applicant — or, in a joint application, either party — applies for the conditional order. This is done through MyHMCTS. There is no additional fee at this stage.

The conditional order is the court's formal confirmation that you are entitled to a divorce. A judge reviews the application and, if satisfied, makes the conditional order. In most straightforward cases, this is dealt with on the papers — you do not attend court. The conditional order is sent to you in writing.

Receiving the conditional order does not mean you are divorced. You are not. The marriage is still legally intact at this point. The conditional order is a milestone in the process, not the end of it.

There is one important point here that often surprises people. Many people wait until their finances are resolved before applying for the final order, even though they are entitled to apply six weeks and one day after the conditional order regardless of whether a financial settlement is in place. The reason this matters is practical and significant: if one spouse dies after the divorce is finalised but before a financial order is in place, the surviving spouse loses their status as a spouse — which can affect pension entitlements, widow or widower benefits, inheritance rights, and the ability to bring certain financial claims. Applying for the final order before those matters are resolved can have consequences that are difficult or impossible to undo. This is why many people in this position take legal advice specifically on the question of timing before applying for the final order.

---

## Stage 5: The final order

Six weeks and one day after the date of the conditional order, either party can apply for the final order. In a joint application, either party can apply. In a sole application, the applicant applies first; if they do not apply within 12 months of being eligible to do so, the respondent can apply, but will need to explain to the court why the applicant has not done so.

The final order is the document that legally ends your marriage. From the date it is made, you are divorced.

The final order is applied for through MyHMCTS. A judge considers it on the papers. If there is nothing preventing it — no outstanding procedural issues, no jurisdictional dispute — it is granted and issued to both parties.

Keep the final order safely. It is a legal document you may need in the future — for remarriage, for certain financial and administrative matters, and sometimes for pension purposes. If you lose it, you can apply to the court for a copy, but it is easier to keep it from the start.

## What happens if you cannot agree on finances or children

The divorce process itself is separate from any dispute about finances or children. You can complete the divorce even if those matters are not resolved — but as explained above, there are real practical reasons why many people do not apply for the final order until their finances are in order.

If you and your spouse cannot agree on finances, the financial remedy process provides a structured route to resolution, with court hearings and, ultimately, a judge's decision if necessary. That process is explained in full in the financial remedy guide.

If you cannot agree on arrangements for your children, the child arrangements process — which is also entirely separate from the divorce — sets out how those disputes are resolved, starting with mediation and potentially moving through a series of court hearings. That process is explained in the child arrangements guide.

Neither of those processes depends on the divorce being finalised. They can run in parallel.

## A note on legal costs

The no-fault divorce process was designed to reduce conflict and, as a consequence, reduce costs. In most cases, you do not need a solicitor to file for divorce, progress through the stages, and obtain the final order. The process is online, the forms are relatively straightforward, and the MyHMCTS platform guides you through them.

Where costs can escalate is if disputes arise about finances or children. Those are the areas where the stakes of getting things wrong are highest, and where legal assistance is most commonly sought — even by people who have handled the divorce itself without representation.

If you are not entitled to legal aid and cannot afford a solicitor, there are options. Citizens Advice can help with general guidance. The Personal Support Unit provides practical assistance to unrepresented people at some court centres. Law centres in some areas provide free legal advice. The Family Mediation Council's website has a directory of accredited mediators.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need to give a reason for the divorce?**
No. Under the no-fault divorce rules introduced in April 2022, you simply state that the marriage has broken down irretrievably. That statement is accepted by the court. You do not need to prove fault, blame your spouse, or give specific reasons.

**Can my spouse stop the divorce?**
No. A spouse cannot contest the divorce on the grounds that they do not believe the marriage has broken down or that they do not want to be divorced. They can raise issues about jurisdiction — whether England and Wales is the right country to deal with the divorce — but they cannot prevent it on its merits. This is one of the most significant changes the 2020 Act introduced.

**How long does it take?**
The minimum time from filing to final order is around 26 weeks, assuming no delays and both parties cooperating. In practice, many cases take longer — delays in service, applications to the court, and the time taken to resolve financial matters all affect the overall timeline.

**What is the difference between the conditional order and the final order?**
The conditional order is the court's confirmation that there is no reason why you cannot divorce. It is not the divorce itself. The final order — made at least six weeks and one day after the conditional order — is the document that legally ends your marriage. You are not divorced until the final order is made.

**Can I remarry after the conditional order?**
No. You can only remarry after the final order has been made and your marriage is legally ended. Remarrying before the final order would make any new marriage void.

**Does divorce automatically divide our property?**
No. The divorce process has no power to divide property, close off financial claims, or make decisions about pensions or maintenance. Those matters require a separate financial remedy process and a financial order made or approved by the court. Without a financial order, either party can make financial claims against the other in future, potentially years after the divorce.

**Do we have to use MyHMCTS?**
The online route through MyHMCTS is the standard route. In limited circumstances — for example, if you have specific accessibility needs — a paper application may be possible. Contact the court for guidance if the online route is not accessible to you.

**What happens if I change my mind during the 20-week period?**
In many cases an application can be withdrawn, although the procedural position depends on the stage the proceedings have reached. If you are considering withdrawing, it is worth understanding your options at the specific stage you have reached before taking any action.

**What if my ex and I have already separated and sorted out our finances informally?**
An informal agreement — even a written one — is not legally binding. To make a financial agreement enforceable, it must be recorded in a consent order that has been approved by a judge. Without a consent order, either party can make financial claims against the other at any future point. This applies even if you have been separated for years and have long since divided everything between you.

**Why does the timing of the final order matter for finances?**
If one spouse dies after the divorce is finalised but before a financial order is in place, the surviving spouse loses their status as a spouse. This can affect pension entitlements, widow or widower benefits, and inheritance rights — as well as the ability to bring certain financial claims. This is one of the reasons many people in this situation seek legal advice specifically on the question of when to apply for the final order.
