If you and your child's other parent cannot agree on where your child lives or how much time they spend with each of you, you can apply to the family court for a child arrangements order. This guide explains the entire process from start to finish, in plain English, for people who are going through it without a solicitor.

---

### What a child arrangements order is

A child arrangements order is a legal order made under section 8 of the Children Act 1989 that sets out where a child lives, who they spend time with, and when. When deciding whether to make such an order, the court applies the welfare principle in section 1 of the Children Act 1989, which places the child's welfare at the centre of every decision.

The court also applies what is called the no order principle. This means the court will only make an order if doing so is better for the child than making no order at all. It is a question of necessity, not just whether the parties can agree — the court must be satisfied that an order serves the child's interests before it will make one.

The order does not decide who is the better parent. It decides what arrangements will work best for the child. The court is not there to judge you. It is there to work out what is in your child's best interests.

You do not need a solicitor to apply. Thousands of people go through this process every year without legal representation. It is harder without a solicitor, but it is possible, and this guide is written to help you understand exactly what you are walking into.

### Before you can apply: MIAM

Before filing any paperwork with the court, applicants are generally required to attend a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting, known as a MIAM. This is a meeting with a trained mediator who explains what family mediation is and whether it might help in your situation.

The MIAM is not mediation itself. It is a short meeting, usually around an hour, where you find out whether mediation is a realistic option. You attend separately from the other parent.

The law requires applicants either to attend a MIAM or to satisfy one of the statutory exemptions. The court will not accept an application where the MIAM section of the C100 form is blank and no exemption is stated.

The exemptions are more varied than many people expect. They include situations involving domestic abuse — and the evidence required to demonstrate this is broader than police records alone, covering a range of documented indicators — as well as urgency where a child faces immediate risk, cases where the parties have previously attended a MIAM, existing court proceedings, and situations where attendance is practically impossible. If you believe you may qualify for an exemption, the C100 form has a section where you state which one applies.

The MIAM requirement sits within the court's wider duty under Rule 3 of the Family Procedure Rules 2010 to encourage non-court dispute resolution wherever appropriate.

If mediation does not resolve things, or if you are exempt, the mediator signs off your C100 form to confirm you have attended. You can then apply to court.

### The C100 form

The C100 is the application form used to ask the family court to make a child arrangements order. It is available on GOV.UK and can be filed online via MyHMCTS or by post.

The form asks for your details and the other parent's details, the child's details including their current living arrangements, what order you are asking the court to make, and whether there are any safety concerns, including domestic abuse, drug or alcohol issues, or concerns about the child's welfare.

The section on safety is important and should be completed fully and accurately. The court takes this information seriously and it shapes what happens next.

When you file the C100, a court fee is payable. As of 2026, this is £255 for an online application. If you are on a low income, it may be possible to apply for help with fees using form EX160.

In most cases, once the court receives the application it sends a copy to the other parent. There are some exceptions where service arrangements differ, for example in cases involving safety concerns, but in ordinary proceedings the respondent is notified by the court.

### CAFCASS

CAFCASS stands for the Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service. It is a non-departmental public body sponsored by the Ministry of Justice, and it works with the family court to make sure the child's interests are at the centre of any decision.

When a C100 is filed, CAFCASS carries out safeguarding checks on both parties. These involve telephone contact with both parents, checking police records, and checking social services records. CAFCASS is looking for any information that suggests a child may be at risk of harm.

Before the first hearing, CAFCASS writes a safeguarding letter. In most cases this is provided to the court and to both parties, though in some cases where safeguarding concerns arise, disclosure may be restricted. The letter summarises what CAFCASS found in their checks and makes initial observations about the case. It is not a full report. It is a starting point.

If the case is more complex, or if the court decides it needs more information, CAFCASS may be asked to prepare a full Section 7 report later in the proceedings. A Section 7 report is a detailed investigation that includes interviews with both parents, the child depending on their age, and often teachers and other professionals involved in the child's life. It takes several months and results in a written recommendation to the court.

Not every case gets a Section 7 report. Many are resolved before one is needed.

### Domestic abuse and Practice Direction 12J

Where one party raises allegations of domestic abuse or coercive control, the proceedings follow a specific framework set out in Practice Direction 12J of the Family Procedure Rules. Under PD12J, the court must consider at the outset whether the allegations are relevant to the arrangements being sought, and if so, whether a fact-finding hearing is needed before any final order is made.

A fact-finding hearing is a separate hearing at which the court hears evidence and makes findings about whether the alleged abuse took place. The court generally needs to resolve disputed allegations before it can properly decide what future arrangements are in the child's best interests — findings of fact can significantly affect what the court considers appropriate.

Not every case involving allegations of abuse leads to a fact-finding hearing. The court has discretion. But where allegations are disputed and potentially relevant to the child's welfare, it is a realistic possibility in the proceedings.

---

### The first hearing: FHDRA

The First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment, known as the FHDRA, is the first court hearing. It usually takes place around four to eight weeks after the application is filed, though backlogs mean timings vary by court.

The FHDRA has two purposes. First, to see whether the case can be resolved without further hearings. Second, if it cannot, to set out a timetable for what happens next.

At the FHDRA, the judge or magistrates will have read the C100, the safeguarding letter from CAFCASS, and any documents filed by the parties ahead of the hearing. A CAFCASS officer will usually be at court and may speak with both parties before the hearing begins.

Many parties choose to file a short position statement before the hearing. Courts often find these helpful. A position statement is typically no longer than two pages and sets out what the party is asking for and why it is in the child's best interests.

If both parties reach an agreement at the FHDRA, the court can make a consent order on those terms and the case closes. Many cases do resolve at this stage.

If the case does not resolve, the judge will give directions. Directions are instructions about what happens next: whether a Section 7 report is needed, what evidence should be filed, and when the next hearing will be. The court expects those directions to be followed and the deadlines in them to be met.

---

### Between hearings

If the case does not resolve at the FHDRA, there is usually a gap of several months before the next hearing. This is one of the hardest parts of the process.

During this time, parties are expected to comply with the directions the judge gave. Missing a filing deadline without good reason can affect how the court views a party's conduct, and in serious cases can result in evidence being excluded. If a deadline cannot be met, contacting the court and the other party promptly to explain is the appropriate course.

Many parties choose to keep dated records of significant events during proceedings — contact that does or does not take place, relevant communications, and anything that may bear on the child's welfare. A clear, contemporaneous record is generally more useful in proceedings than general statements made from memory.

---

### The Dispute Resolution Appointment

If the case has not resolved and directions have been given for further evidence or a Section 7 report, there will usually be a further hearing once that process is complete. This is sometimes referred to as a Dispute Resolution Appointment, or DRA, though listing practices vary between courts and not every case includes a hearing with that specific label.

By this point, the court has significantly more information. Both parties have filed evidence. CAFCASS has made a recommendation. The judge has a clearer picture of the case.

This hearing is another opportunity to reach agreement. Many cases settle at this stage.

If they do not, the court will list the case for a final hearing.

---

### The final hearing

The final hearing is where a judge hears the full evidence and makes a decision. It is the least common outcome — most cases resolve before reaching this point — but it is important to understand what it involves.

At a final hearing, both parties give oral evidence. This means answering questions about written statements, and potentially being cross-examined by the other party or their legal representative. CAFCASS may also give evidence.

The judge will apply the welfare checklist set out in section 1 of the Children Act 1989. This is the list of factors the court must consider, including the child's wishes and feelings depending on their age and understanding, their physical and emotional needs, the likely effect of any change in circumstances, and any risk of harm.

The judge makes an order. That order is legally binding on both parties.

---

### What a child arrangements order says

A child arrangements order sets out, in practical terms, what happens. It will typically specify where the child lives, how much time the child spends with the other parent including during school holidays, and how contact happens in practice — including whether it is supervised or unsupervised and how handovers take place.

The order can be detailed or relatively brief, depending on the case. Once made, it generally remains in force until the child turns 16, though in some circumstances orders can continue until the child is 18. Either party can apply to vary the order if circumstances change significantly.

---

### If the order is not followed

The court has a range of powers to enforce a child arrangements order where one party fails to comply without reasonable excuse. These include requiring attendance at a separated parents information programme, imposing an unpaid work requirement, varying the order, and in serious and repeated cases, more significant enforcement action. The enforcement framework is set out in the Children Act 1989 and the court considers the circumstances of any breach before deciding what, if any, action to take.

If the other party is consistently not complying with an order, an application can be made back to court using form C79.

---

### How long does this take

The honest answer is: longer than it should. In 2024, the average private law child arrangements case took 43 weeks from application to disposal. Some cases take considerably longer, particularly those involving a Section 7 report, a fact-finding hearing, or a final hearing.

The rough timeline is: MIAM before the application is filed; C100 filing to FHDRA around four to eight weeks, often longer due to backlogs; FHDRA to the next hearing around three to six months if further evidence or a Section 7 report is directed; and a further two to four months if the case proceeds to a final hearing after that.

The court expects parties to comply with filing deadlines and to engage constructively with any opportunities to resolve the case without a final hearing.

---

### Do you need a solicitor

You are not required to have a solicitor. Many people go through this process without one. But it is worth being clear about what that means in practice.

A solicitor knows the procedural rules, can draft documents correctly, and can advocate in hearings. If the other party has a solicitor and you do not, there is an imbalance, and it can be significant in a hearing room.

Legal aid for private family law cases has been severely restricted since 2013. In 2024, around 80% of private family cases had at least one unrepresented party. Options worth knowing about include limited scope representation, where a solicitor assists with specific documents or hearings rather than the whole case, McKenzie friend support at hearings, and free services such as Citizens Advice and Support Through Court.

### FAQ

**Do I have to go to a MIAM before I can apply to court?**

In most cases, yes. Before filing a C100, applicants are generally required to attend a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting. There are statutory exemptions, including situations involving domestic abuse, urgency, previous MIAM attendance, and other specific circumstances. The evidence required to demonstrate a domestic abuse exemption is broader than police records alone. If an exemption applies, it is stated on the C100 form. The court will not process an application where the MIAM requirement is not addressed.

**What does CAFCASS actually do in my case?**

CAFCASS carries out safeguarding checks on both parties before the first hearing, including checks with police and social services. They produce a safeguarding letter for the court, which in most cases is also provided to both parties. In more complex cases, the court may ask CAFCASS to prepare a full Section 7 report, which involves interviewing both parents and the child and results in a recommendation about what arrangements would best serve the child's welfare.

**How long does a child arrangements case take?**

In 2024, the average private law case took 43 weeks from application to disposal. Cases that resolve at the first hearing are shorter. Cases involving a Section 7 report, a fact-finding hearing, or a final hearing take longer. Court backlogs have extended timelines significantly over the past decade.

**What is a position statement and do I need one?**

A position statement is a short document, usually no more than two pages, setting out what a party is asking for and why it is in the child's best interests. Many parties choose to file one ahead of the FHDRA and courts often find them useful. It is not the place to list every grievance — a focused, child-centred document is generally more useful to the court than a lengthy account of the relationship breakdown.

**What happens if the other parent does not follow the order?**

The court has a range of enforcement powers under the Children Act 1989 where a party fails to comply with a child arrangements order without reasonable excuse. These include requiring attendance at a separated parents information programme, imposing an unpaid work requirement, varying the order, and in serious cases more significant action. An application to enforce can be made using form C79. The court considers the circumstances of any breach before deciding what action is appropriate.

**Can the child decide where they live?**

A child does not have the legal right to decide, but their wishes and feelings are one of the factors the court must consider under the Children Act 1989 welfare checklist. How much weight is given to those views depends on the child's age and level of understanding. There is no fixed age at which a child's views become determinative, but the views of older children are generally given significant weight in practice.

**What is a Section 7 report?**

A Section 7 report is a detailed welfare report prepared by CAFCASS, or in some cases a local authority, when the court needs more information to make a decision. It involves interviews with both parents and usually the child, and may include input from schools or other professionals. The report ends with a recommendation to the court about what arrangements would best serve the child's welfare. The court is not bound by that recommendation but generally takes it seriously.

**Do I need a solicitor to go through this process?**

No. It is possible to represent yourself throughout. The process is harder without legal representation, particularly at hearings, but many people do it. Options for those who cannot access legal aid or afford full representation include limited scope advice from a solicitor, McKenzie friend support, and free services like Support Through Court.
