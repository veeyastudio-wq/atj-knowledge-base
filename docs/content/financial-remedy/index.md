# The financial remedy process in England and Wales — what happens, in what order, if you don't have a solicitor

When a marriage or civil partnership ends, the divorce itself and the division of finances are two separate legal processes. The divorce deals with the legal status of your relationship. Financial remedy deals with everything you own, owe, and earn — and how that gets divided between you. One does not automatically sort out the other.

This guide covers the financial remedy track from beginning to end: what each stage is, what you are expected to do, and what happens if you cannot agree. It is written for people going through this without a solicitor. It will not tell you what outcome to accept or what to ask for. That decision belongs to you. What it will do is make sure you understand the process before you are standing in the middle of it.

---

## First, a word on timing

Financial remedy runs alongside divorce, not after it. Most people assume they need to finish the divorce before dealing with money. They do not. In fact, it is usually better to start the financial remedy process early, because it takes time, and because waiting until after the divorce is finalised can close off certain claims under the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973.

The financial remedy process has six main stages: MIAM, Form A, Form E, the First Appointment, the Financial Dispute Resolution hearing (known as the FDR), and — if you have not settled by then — a final hearing. Most cases settle before reaching the final hearing. Many settle at or before the FDR.

---

## Stage 1: MIAM — mediation information and assessment meeting

Before you can apply to the court for a financial remedy, you are required by the Family Procedure Rules 2010 to attend a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting, known as a MIAM. This is a meeting with an accredited family mediator — usually lasting around 45 minutes — where mediation is explained to you and a mediator assesses whether your case is suitable for it.

A MIAM is not mediation itself. It is an assessment of whether mediation might work. You and your ex do not attend together. You each attend separately.

If the mediator decides your case is not suitable for mediation — or if you fall into one of the categories that are exempt from the MIAM requirement — they will sign a document confirming this. You will need that document to make your court application. If your ex simply refuses to attend their own MIAM, the mediator can confirm this in writing, and you can proceed to make your court application on that basis. You do not need your ex's cooperation to move forward.

**Exemptions exist.** Domestic abuse is the most significant one. If there is a history of domestic abuse between you and your ex, you may not be required to attend a MIAM. Other exemptions include situations where your ex cannot be located, where there is a risk to life, and where the other party has already confirmed they are unwilling to attend. The full list of exemptions is set out in the Family Procedure Rules 2010, Practice Direction 3A.

The MIAM is not a formality to get through as quickly as possible. If mediation is available and appropriate, it can resolve your financial situation faster and with less cost and conflict than the court route. It is worth going into the meeting genuinely open to that possibility.

## Stage 2: Form A — starting the court process

If mediation does not work, is not suitable, or your ex will not engage, you apply to the court by filing Form A — the application for a financial order. This is the document that formally starts the financial remedy process inside the court system.

Form A is filed online through MyHMCTS, the court's case management portal. When you file it, you pay an application fee. **Before you apply, check GOV.UK for the current figure** — court fees are reviewed periodically and the amount may have changed. The court then issues a timetable, which sets out the key deadlines for both parties.

Once Form A is filed, both parties are given a date for the First Appointment hearing, typically 12 to 16 weeks after the application. The timetable also sets the deadline for exchanging financial disclosure — which is where Form E comes in.

---

## Stage 3: Form E — financial disclosure

Form E is the financial disclosure document that both parties must complete and exchange before the First Appointment. It is, in the experience of most unrepresented people, the hardest part of the entire process.

It is a comprehensive statement of everything you own, everything you owe, and everything you earn. Property. Bank accounts. Savings and investments. Pensions. Business interests. Debts and liabilities. Income from every source. Future financial needs. Contributions to the marriage — financial and otherwise. It runs to 28 sections and must be signed as a statement of truth. That means you are confirming, under penalty of perjury, that the information you have provided is accurate and complete.

**Why Form E is hard without a solicitor.** The form itself is long and some sections are technical. Pension valuations require a specific figure called the Cash Equivalent Transfer Value (CETV), which you have to request from your pension provider — they are legally required to provide it, but it can take time. If you have a business, you may need accounts or a valuation. If you own property, you need a current market value, typically from a recent estate agent's appraisal or a surveyor. Some sections ask you to estimate future needs, which feels speculative but matters — particularly housing needs for yourself and any children.

**Disclosure must be complete.** The court takes non-disclosure seriously. If your ex fails to disclose assets, the court has powers to draw adverse inferences from that failure — in other words, to assume they are hiding something and to factor that into the outcome. If you fail to disclose something, the same applies to you. A financial order made on the basis of incomplete disclosure can be set aside later.

Both parties exchange Form E simultaneously, on the deadline set by the court. You send yours on the same day your ex sends theirs. Neither party sees the other's form before that date.

After exchange, you have time to review what your ex has disclosed and prepare for the First Appointment. If something looks incomplete or unclear, you can request further information — this is called a questionnaire, and you take it to the First Appointment.

---

## Stage 4: the First Appointment

The First Appointment — sometimes abbreviated to FA or FDA (First Directions Appointment) — is the first time both parties appear before a judge. It is not a hearing where the court makes a decision about how your finances will be divided. It is a case management hearing. The judge's job at this stage is to get the case properly organised for the next stage.

At the First Appointment, the judge will:

- Review the financial disclosure and identify any gaps or issues
- Deal with any questionnaires either party has prepared
- Make directions — these are instructions about what each party must do before the FDR, and by when
- Consider whether any valuations or expert reports are needed (for property, pensions, or businesses)
- In some cases, explore whether the case can settle then and there

You will be expected to attend in person or via video link, depending on the court. You will be expected to have read your ex's Form E and to know your own. If you have a questionnaire — a list of questions about your ex's disclosure — you need to bring it. If you need the court to order something specific before the FDR, this is the moment to say so.

The First Appointment can feel overwhelming if you are not prepared. The practical preparation is straightforward: know your Form E numbers, read your ex's Form E, note anything that looks missing or unexplained, and be clear about what questions you want answered before the next stage.

---

## Stage 5: the FDR — Financial Dispute Resolution hearing

The FDR is the most important hearing in most financial remedy cases. A large proportion of cases settle at or before the FDR. It is specifically designed to be a negotiation hearing, and the judge who presides over it plays a different role than a judge at a final hearing.

At the FDR, the judge will give an indication — sometimes called a "view" — of what they think is a fair outcome based on what they have seen. This is not binding. The judge who gives the indication cannot be the judge who decides the case if it goes to a final hearing. The whole point is to give both parties a realistic assessment of where they stand, so that settlement becomes possible.

Before the FDR, both parties must file a summary of their positions — what they are proposing as a settlement. These are called offers, and they are made "without prejudice" at this stage. Without prejudice means that if the case does not settle, neither party can show those offers to the judge at the final hearing. The protection exists to encourage genuine negotiation. If the case does settle and a consent order is drafted, the offers can be referenced in drawing up that agreement — the without-prejudice protection applies only to a contested final hearing, not to a negotiated resolution.

On the day of the FDR, there is usually time before the hearing for the parties to negotiate. Sometimes a settlement is reached in the corridor before either party goes before the judge. Sometimes the judge's indication during the hearing prompts a settlement immediately afterwards. Sometimes it does not settle and the case is listed for a final hearing.

If you are unrepresented, the FDR can feel particularly high-stakes because you will be negotiating directly with your ex or their solicitor without legal support in the room. It is worth knowing that you are entitled to take time to consider any offer made — you do not have to accept or refuse immediately. It is also worth knowing that the court has discretion to make a costs order against a party who has behaved unreasonably — including at the FDR stage. Refusing a reasonable offer without good reason, or taking positions that have no realistic prospect of success, can be factors the court takes into account if costs are argued later.

---

## Stage 6: the final hearing

If the case has not settled by the end of the FDR, it is listed for a final hearing. This is where a judge hears evidence from both parties, considers the full financial picture, and makes a binding decision about how the finances are divided.

The final hearing is a formal court proceeding. Both parties give evidence under oath. The judge will ask questions. If your ex has legal representation, their solicitor or barrister may cross-examine you. You have the right to cross-examine your ex.

Before the final hearing, there will be further directions — a schedule of what needs to be filed and when. Both parties are usually required to file updated financial disclosure, a statement of issues, a chronology of the relationship, a schedule of assets, and a statement of their position on what the outcome should be.

The final hearing is the least common outcome. It is also the most costly and stressful one. The court's expectation — and the design of the process — is that most cases will settle before they get here. That said, final hearings do happen, and if they are necessary, going through one without legal representation is genuinely difficult but not impossible. If you are facing a final hearing without a solicitor, it is worth looking into free or low-cost support options — McKenzie Friends can attend with you and provide quiet assistance, and the Personal Support Unit provides help to unrepresented parties at some court centres — check whether it is available at your court before the hearing, as coverage varies. Preparation is everything.

## What the court is actually considering

Whether your case settles or goes to a final hearing, it helps to understand what the court would apply if it were making the decision. This is not advice about what you should accept. It is information about the legal framework that shapes every financial remedy outcome.

Under the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, section 25, the court must consider all the circumstances of the case. The first consideration is always the welfare of any child of the family who is under 18. Beyond that, the court weighs a list of factors that includes:

- The income, earning capacity, property, and financial resources of each party — now and in the foreseeable future
- The financial needs, obligations, and responsibilities of each party
- The standard of living during the marriage
- The age of each party and the length of the marriage
- Any physical or mental disability of either party
- The contributions each party has made to the welfare of the family, including looking after the home and caring for children
- The conduct of each party, but only where it would be inequitable to disregard it — in practice, this is a high bar; conduct rarely affects the financial outcome unless it is exceptional, and everyday relationship breakdown almost never meets it
- The value of any benefit — including pension — that either party will lose because of the divorce

The court's aim, in most cases involving modest assets, is fairness. Where the assets are sufficient, the starting point is often an equal division, adjusted for the circumstances above. But fairness is not a formula, and outcomes vary significantly depending on the facts of each case.

One thing worth knowing if you have read about family law more broadly: unlike in children proceedings, there is no "no order" principle in financial remedy. In children cases, the court will only make an order if doing so is better than making no order at all. In financial remedy, the court has a wide discretion to make whatever order it considers appropriate and is not subject to the same presumption. The process is about reaching a fair financial outcome, not about whether an order should be made at all.

## A note on settlement

Most financial remedy cases settle before a final hearing — many at or before the FDR. Settlement is not giving up. It is reaching an agreement that both parties can live with, without handing the decision to a judge.

If you do settle, the agreement needs to be recorded in a consent order — a formal court document that makes your agreement binding. A consent order must be approved by a judge. Without one, an agreement between you and your ex is not legally enforceable, and either of you could make a financial claim against the other in future, even years later.

Do not confuse "we've agreed" with "we're done." The consent order is the final step, and it matters.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I have to go to mediation before applying to court?**
In most cases, yes. You are required to attend a MIAM before making a court application for a financial order. The mediator assesses whether your case is suitable for mediation and signs a document if it is not. There are exemptions — domestic abuse is the most significant — but the default is that a MIAM must happen first.

**Can I start financial remedy proceedings before the divorce is finalised?**
Yes. Financial remedy runs alongside divorce, not after it. You can start the process at any point after filing for divorce, and in most cases it makes sense to do so. Waiting until after the divorce is finalised can create complications and may close off certain claims.

**What happens if my ex refuses to fill in Form E or hides assets?**
The court takes non-disclosure seriously. If your ex fails to provide full disclosure, the court can draw adverse inferences — it can assume they have more than they are showing and make an order that reflects that. In serious cases, the court has additional powers to order disclosure, instruct forensic accountants, and in extreme cases deal with the matter as contempt of court.

**Do I need a solicitor to go through this process?**
You are entitled to represent yourself throughout. Many people do. The process is procedurally demanding — particularly Form E — but it is designed to be navigable without legal representation. That said, having a solicitor review a proposed settlement or draft a consent order is worth considering, even if you have handled everything else yourself. A consent order that is poorly drafted can create problems later.

**What is a clean break order?**
A clean break order is a financial order that severs all financial ties between you and your ex. Once made, neither of you can make any further financial claim against the other. Not all cases result in a clean break — it depends on the circumstances, including whether ongoing maintenance is appropriate. But where a clean break is achievable, it provides finality that an informal agreement cannot.

**How long does financial remedy take?**
It depends on the complexity of the case and whether it settles. A straightforward case that settles before or at the FDR might be resolved within six to twelve months of filing Form A. A contested case that goes to a final hearing can take eighteen months or longer. The court's general expectation is that the process from Form A to FDR is around six months.

**What is the difference between a consent order and a financial order?**
A financial order is any court order dealing with the division of finances — it could be made by a judge after a contested final hearing, or by the court approving an agreement the parties have reached themselves. A consent order is a specific type of financial order that records an agreement between the parties. Both are legally binding. An informal written agreement between you and your ex — without court approval — is not.

**What happens at the FDR if we cannot agree?**
If the FDR does not result in settlement, the judge who heard the FDR steps out of the case entirely — they cannot preside over the final hearing. A new judge is assigned, and the case proceeds to a final hearing where a binding decision is made. Offers made on a without-prejudice basis during the FDR process cannot be shown to the final hearing judge. However, if a party made an open offer — one explicitly marked as open rather than without prejudice — that offer can be referred to at the final hearing, and is often relevant to whether the court makes a costs order against either party.
