## The strategic context

ATJ publishes legal information for people navigating the UK family court system without a lawyer. Every page on the site sits inside Google's highest-scrutiny category: Your Money or Your Life (YMYL). Legal content falls explicitly within it.

That changes what SEO means for this product. It is not primarily about ranking tricks. It is about being a trusted, credible, human-authored source that Google and AI systems choose to surface when someone who is frightened and alone searches for help at midnight.

There are two distinct things happening in search in 2026 that ATJ must plan for simultaneously.

The first is Google's AI Overviews, which appear above traditional results for a large proportion of queries, synthesise answers from multiple sources, and then optionally link through. Being cited inside an AI Overview now matters more than being ranked first. Sites cited as sources in AI Overviews see a traffic increase; sites whose results are absorbed into an AI Overview without citation see their click-through rate fall sharply.

The second is AI tools like ChatGPT and Perplexity, where people now ask full natural-language questions and receive answers drawn from crawled sources. For someone asking "what happens when CAFCASS contacts me", ATJ should eventually be one of those sources.

Both require the same underlying thing: authoritative, human-authored, clearly structured, regularly updated content that AI systems are confident enough to cite.

## The non-negotiable foundation: authorship and credentials

Before a single article is published, this decision must be locked.

**Vilam Vuong is the named author of all ATJ content.** Not "the ATJ team". Not anonymous. Not a pen name. Vilam Vuong, with a brief biography that states plainly: he navigated the UK family court system himself, this product was built from that experience, and every piece of content is written from the inside, not from a distance.

**Every article must also carry a solicitor review note.** Not that it was written by a solicitor. That it was reviewed by a named solicitor for accuracy against current law and procedure. The solicitor's name, their SRA number, and the review date must appear on the page. This is not optional for YMYL content. Google's quality rater guidelines are explicit: anonymous legal content, or legal content without verifiable credentials, will not rank in this category regardless of its quality.

This is what the solicitor consultation already on the horizon must produce as a deliverable: not just sign-off on the system prompt, but a named-reviewer relationship for the content programme.

**Why this matters beyond compliance:** Vilam's personal experience is the product's actual E-E-A-T signal for the Experience dimension of Google's framework. No competitor can manufacture this. Caira by Unwildered is a legal chatbot. It has no founding story and no named author. ATJ's human origin is structural differentiation, and it must be visible on every page.

The user is not typing "litigant in person procedure England and Wales". They are typing what they are actually experiencing. Some examples, grounded in how this audience actually searches:

- "Form E what do I have to include"
- "what happens if I miss a court deadline"
- "FDR hearing what to expect"

Every article in this programme starts with: what exact question does this answer? If the answer is not a question a real person would type into Google or ask an AI, the article does not belong in the programme yet.

## Content architecture: pillar pages and clusters

### The structure

ATJ's content is organised around the three tracks of the family court journey, which are also the three areas where unrepresented people are most likely to search for help.

Each track has one **pillar page**: a comprehensive, plain-English guide to the entire track. Long, definitive, deeply linked. The pillar page is not trying to rank for a competitive head term. It is the authoritative hub that establishes ATJ's topical authority across the cluster.

Around each pillar sits a **cluster of 8 to 12 articles**, each answering one specific question from the real person going through this stage. Narrow, focused, 1,200 to 2,000 words each. Every cluster article links back to its pillar. The pillar links to every cluster article. This structure is what tells Google that ATJ owns this subject, not just that it has published one article about it.

**Cluster 1: Child arrangements**

Pillar: "Applying for a child arrangements order in England and Wales — a complete guide for people without a solicitor"

**Cluster 2: Financial remedy**

### Why this structure and not another

Single articles are not enough in a YMYL category. Google needs to see that ATJ covers the subject comprehensively before it trusts any one piece of content. The pillar-and-cluster model is how topical authority is built and signalled. It also means that someone who lands on the CAFCASS article and wants to understand the wider process has somewhere obvious to go: the child arrangements pillar. Internal linking is not decoration. It is how authority flows through the site and how users navigate a journey.

Sites that sustain cluster publishing for 12 or more months see meaningfully higher organic traffic than single-page approaches. This is a compounding channel, not a campaign.

## Content format requirements

These are not style preferences. They are structural requirements for YMYL legal content that needs to perform in AI search.

**Lead with the answer.** Every article opens with a direct, plain-English answer to the question in the title. Not a preamble. Not "in this article we will explore." The answer. Two to four sentences. This is how you get cited in an AI Overview: give the AI something it can extract cleanly in the first paragraph.

**Verified legal accuracy only.** No hedging invented facts. Every procedural claim cites the specific rule or form it comes from. Outdated legal content is classified as harmful by Google. Every article must carry a last-reviewed date and must be updated when the procedure it covers changes.

**FAQ section on every article.** Five to eight questions that a real user would ask as follow-up after reading the piece. Each question answered in 40 to 80 words. This format is disproportionately effective for AI citation and for Google's People Also Ask feature. The questions should come from real searches and real discovery conversations, not from editorial invention.

**Plain language throughout.** Legal terms must be explained when first used. The reader is not a lawyer. They are a frightened person who has just received a court letter. The reading level should be accessible to someone with no legal background. This is both an ethical requirement and an SEO one: content that confuses the reader fails on engagement signals.

**Schema markup.** Every article carries FAQPage schema on the FAQ section, and Article schema on the page itself (including author entity, publish date, and modified date). This is not required for AI Overviews, but it is a trust signal for rich results and it gives both Google and AI systems structured, machine-readable metadata about who wrote the article, when, and when it was last reviewed. The CMS must generate this automatically — it should not be a manual task per article.

**Source citations.** Every procedural claim links to its source: the FPR, the relevant form on GOV.UK, an HMCTS practice direction. Not as academic footnotes. As plain-text links ("under the Family Procedure Rules 2010" links to the FPR). This is a trust signal for both Google and AI systems.

**Author block and review note.** On every article. Vilam Vuong's name, biography, and the date the article was reviewed by a named solicitor. The solicitor's name and SRA number. No exceptions.

**Published date and last-reviewed date.** Both visible. Perplexity weights content recency heavily. Outdated content without a review date is deprioritised by AI systems.

## The TikTok to article pipeline

ATJ already has 184 TikTok scripts across seven content layers. This is not a separate channel from the SEO programme. It is the research and ideation layer of it.

The pipeline works as follows:

Every TikTok script that answers a question falling within one of the three content clusters maps directly to a cluster article. The script becomes the research skeleton. The article expands on it with verified sources, a FAQ section, an author block, and internal links.

This means the content programme does not start from a blank page. It starts from 184 scripts representing real questions from the audience this product is built for. The editing work is expansion and verification, not invention.

This pipeline also has a second function: TikTok videos on topics that perform well are signals about which cluster articles to prioritise. High engagement on a CAFCASS video means the CAFCASS article is a priority. The social channel informs the SEO programme's sequencing.

## AI search visibility

Beyond Google, ATJ needs to be visible in ChatGPT and Perplexity responses. This is a separate but related goal.

AI tools build citations from consensus across multiple independent sources. If ATJ appears consistently in legal information publications, on relevant resource lists, in press coverage about the access to justice gap, and on its own site, AI systems gain confidence in citing it.

**The practical steps once the site is live:**

Once a month, open ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Mode and ask the questions from the cluster article list above. Note which sources are cited. Note whether ATJ is cited. The gap between what is cited and what ATJ has published is the content priority list.

**What not to do:** Do not attempt to engineer AI citations through artificial link schemes, paid placements, or by manufacturing mentions across low-quality directories. AI systems are built to detect consensus from genuinely independent sources. Manufactured consensus reads as noise. The only reliable path is publishing authoritative content and earning coverage through genuine merit.

**Technical requirements for AI crawlability:**
- AI crawlers (GPTBot, PerplexityBot) must not be blocked in robots.txt
- Server-side rendering required for all content: AI crawlers do not execute JavaScript reliably
- Every page must carry a visible publication date
- HTTPS throughout

## Earned coverage: the borrowed authority strategy

Organic search and AI citation both benefit from third-party coverage. A single article in The Guardian about the legal aid gap, citing ATJ as a resource, is worth more for citation authority than 50 directory listings.

The coverage strategy has one qualification: Vilam's background as a dental and beauty AI product builder is not the credential for this audience. It creates the wrong associations. The credential for this audience is personal experience of the court system.

**Target coverage categories:**

- Journalism covering the legal aid crisis, the family court backlog, and access to justice (Guardian, Times, BBC, Telegraph family law coverage)
- Family law publications and the Resolution member network
- Charities and civil society organisations working in the access to justice space (Support Through Court, Rights of Women, Gingerbread, Families Need Fathers)
- Parliamentary coverage: the access to justice gap appears regularly in Commons debates, and a briefing note citing ATJ as a resource is a high-authority signal

**The pitch:** ATJ is not a product press release. It is a response to a documented gap that affects hundreds of thousands of people a year. The pitch to journalists and organisations is the problem — 80% of family court cases involving at least one unrepresented party — and the personal story behind the product. The product itself follows from that.

This outreach does not wait for launch. It begins now, or as soon as there is something concrete to point to — a published pillar page, a waitlist, a written explainer of the problem ATJ is solving. Being written about as "a product in development addressing the legal aid gap" six months before launch is worth more than a product announcement the day it goes live. The access to justice gap is a story that runs regardless of whether ATJ exists yet. ATJ's role in that story is the angle.

## What we explicitly do not do

These are decisions, not omissions.

**No unreviewed AI-assisted content.** AI tools may assist with drafting, research, and structure. But every article must be written, edited, and owned by Vilam, and reviewed by a named solicitor before publication. The Experience dimension of E-E-A-T — first-hand knowledge — cannot come from AI-generated prose. It comes from the author's genuine experience of the system being described. Articles that read as AI-generated filler, that carry no authorial voice, no first-hand perspective, and no verifiable expertise signal, will not rank in YMYL categories regardless of technical optimisation. The solicitor review is also a regulatory point: publishing legal information without human review and authorship accountability creates liability questions the product does not need.

**No keyword stuffing or manufactured search optimisation.** Content is written for the reader. Keyword considerations inform topic selection and article titles, not the prose itself.

**No link schemes.** No paid placements, link exchanges, or manufactured directory listings. Google's systems detect these and penalise them. The only links worth having are links earned through the quality of the content.

**No content without solicitor review.** The product's regulatory boundary (information, not advice) applies to the site's content programme as much as it applies to the product itself. Unreviewed legal information published on the ATJ site creates the same risks as unreviewed legal information generated by Talia.

**No content that crosses into advice.** The same line that governs Talia's responses governs the content programme. Articles explain what happens, what options exist, what a form requires. They do not tell the reader what to do in their specific case.

## Sequencing: what happens before launch versus after

### Before launch

1. **Establish the author and reviewer infrastructure.** Vilam's author biography finalised. Solicitor reviewer engaged with an ongoing content review arrangement, not a one-time consult. Author block and review note templates built into the CMS.

2. **Publish the three pillar pages.** These are the anchor content. The clusters build around them. Publishing the pillars before launch means they have index time before any user traffic arrives.

3. **Publish the first six cluster articles** — two per cluster, prioritised by the questions most likely to be searched first in a person's journey. For child arrangements: the C100 article and the CAFCASS article. For financial remedy: the Form E overview and the First Appointment article. For divorce: how to start the application and what the conditional order is.

4. **Set up Google Search Console and submit the sitemap.** This is not optional. It is how ATJ tells Google the site exists and where to start crawling.

5. **Ensure robots.txt does not block AI crawlers.** This is a technical check, not an ongoing process.

### After launch — the ongoing cadence

Two new articles per month minimum. Priority sequencing determined by:
- TikTok video performance (high-engagement topics become articles first)
- Discovery conversation findings (what questions are people actually asking)
- Search Console data (what queries are landing on ATJ pages, what queries are not yet covered)
- Monthly AI search audit (what is ChatGPT and Perplexity citing for the key queries, what is ATJ not yet covering)

All existing articles reviewed for accuracy every six months and after any change to family court procedure.

## What success looks like and when

This channel does not produce results in weeks. The honest timeline is three to six months before meaningful keyword movement, twelve to eighteen months before the channel is a significant traffic source.

What to track:

- **Google Search Console impressions and clicks** for the cluster keywords. Not volume. Coverage: are the articles appearing for the queries they were written for.
- **AI Overview citation rate**: monthly manual audit across the cluster topic list. Is ATJ being cited or not.
- **Inbound links from credible sources**: not directory noise. Named coverage in publications and organisations ATJ would want to be associated with.
- **Discovery conversation quality**: are people arriving at discovery interviews having already found ATJ through search. This is the earliest signal that the content programme is reaching the right people.

Success is not traffic volume. Success is being the source that a frightened parent at midnight finds when they ask Google or ChatGPT what is going to happen to them in court next week, and finding a clear, credible, human answer.

## Dependencies on the rest of the product build

This spec cannot execute in isolation.

**The solicitor consultation must produce a content review arrangement**, not just sign-off on the system prompt and data retention policy. Without a named reviewer, no content can be published under YMYL compliance requirements.

**The site needs a CMS with specific structural capabilities before the first article goes live.** These are not nice-to-haves: author blocks with biography and photo, named reviewer field with SRA number, publish date and last-reviewed date (both visible on the page), FAQ schema auto-generated from FAQ sections, Article schema auto-generated from page metadata, and sitemap auto-submitted to Google Search Console on publish. If the CMS cannot do these things, the content programme cannot comply with YMYL requirements. This is a prerequisite, not a follow-on task.

**The TikTok content programme must continue in parallel** — it is the research and audience pipeline for the SEO content programme. Pausing TikTok pauses the input signal for which topics to prioritise.

**Discovery conversations must continue** — they are the primary source of what questions real users are actually asking in plain language. The content programme degrades if discovery stops.
