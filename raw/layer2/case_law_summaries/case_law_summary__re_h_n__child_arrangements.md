# Re H-N and Others — domestic abuse allegations in child arrangements proceedings

## What was decided

The Court of Appeal heard four appeals in child arrangements cases where one party had alleged domestic abuse. In each case, the lower court had required the alleging party to set out their allegations as a list of separate incidents — a document called a Scott Schedule. The Court of Appeal found that this approach was often inadequate for cases involving patterns of coercive or controlling behaviour, which by their nature cannot be fairly represented as a list of individual incidents. The court gave guidance on how domestic abuse allegations should be handled in private law children proceedings going forward.

## What principle it established

When domestic abuse is alleged in child arrangements proceedings, the court's primary question is whether the evidence shows a pattern of coercive or controlling behaviour, not simply whether specific incidents can be proved one by one. A judge must consider the overall nature and effect of the behaviour, not treat each allegation in isolation. The court also confirmed that Practice Direction 12J — which sets out how courts must approach domestic abuse allegations in children cases — is mandatory. Courts cannot ignore or minimise it. Where a fact-finding hearing is needed, it should be conducted in a way that allows the full context of the alleged behaviour to be assessed.

## Which track it applies to

Child arrangements. This case applies only where domestic abuse is alleged as part of the proceedings. It is not relevant to child arrangements cases where no such allegations are made.

## When you are likely to encounter it

If you have made allegations of domestic abuse, or allegations have been made against you, this case is likely to be relevant from the FHDRA onwards. At the FHDRA the court will decide whether a fact-finding hearing is needed. If it is, Re H-N shapes how that hearing is conducted — including whether a Scott Schedule will be required and how the allegations will be examined. Cafcass will also reference domestic abuse considerations in their safeguarding checks, which happen before the FHDRA.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

If you are alleging domestic abuse, this case supports presenting a full account of how the other party's behaviour affected you and your children over time, rather than reducing it to a list of incidents with dates. Courts are required to look at the pattern of behaviour, not just individual events. If you have been asked to complete a Scott Schedule, you are entitled to provide context alongside each entry. If allegations have been made against you, the same principle applies in reverse — the court will look at the overall picture, not just whether individual allegations can be disputed in isolation. In either situation, written statements that explain the full history of the relationship clearly are more useful than lists.
