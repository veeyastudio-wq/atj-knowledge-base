# White v White — the yardstick of equality in financial remedy

## What was decided

Martin and Pamela White had farmed together in partnership throughout their 33-year marriage. When the marriage ended, their total assets were approximately £4.6 million. The lower courts awarded Mrs White significantly less than half, reasoning that her reasonable financial needs were met. The House of Lords overturned the approach. The court held that financial need alone was not the ceiling for an award, and that the contribution of a homemaker or non-earning spouse must be treated as equal to that of the breadwinner.

## What principle it established

Before finalising any proposed division of assets, a judge should check that division against what an equal split would produce. If the proposed award gives one party substantially less than half, the judge must identify a good reason for that departure — not simply assume that the lower-earning party needs less. This check is known as the yardstick of equality. It does not mean every case ends in a 50/50 split, but it means equality is the reference point the court measures any other outcome against.

## Which track it applies to

Financial remedy.

## When you are likely to encounter it

White v White is cited in almost every contested financial remedy case. You are most likely to encounter it at the FDR hearing, when the judge gives an indication of how they view the case, and at the final hearing, when the division of assets is decided. If the other party or their solicitor is arguing that you should receive significantly less than half, White v White is the case that establishes why that requires justification.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

Before this case, courts often focused on what each party reasonably needed, which frequently meant the lower-earning spouse — usually the one who had taken on childcare or domestic responsibilities — received less simply because their needs were assessed as lower. White v White changed that. The unpaid contribution to a marriage is treated as equally valuable to the financial contribution. Knowing this matters because it affects how you present your own contribution to the marriage when making or responding to financial arguments in court.
