# Witness statement — giving your evidence to the court in writing

## What it is

A witness statement is a formal written document containing the evidence a party wants to place before the court. Unlike a position statement, which sets out what a party is seeking, a witness statement contains the facts the party is relying on to support that position. Once filed, the witness statement stands as the party's evidence-in-chief — the other party and, where permitted, the CAFCASS officer can cross-examine the author on its contents at a hearing. It must be signed with a statement of truth.

## When it happens

A witness statement is ordered by the court in its directions — typically in the order made at the FHDRA, DRA, or FDR, setting out what is required before the final hearing or a fact-finding hearing. The directions order will specify the deadline for filing and serving the statement and whether there is a word or page limit. Witness statements are not routinely required at interlocutory hearings (hearings dealing with procedural matters only). They are standard before final hearings and fact-finding hearings in both child arrangements and financial remedy proceedings.

## What it involves

**Format.** A witness statement must be in the first person, dated, and signed. Each paragraph must be numbered consecutively. The statement must set out the source of any fact not within the witness's own direct knowledge (for example, something told to the witness by a third party).

**The statement of truth.** At the end of the statement, immediately before the signature, the witness must include a statement of truth in the prescribed form: "I believe that the facts stated in this witness statement are true. I understand that proceedings for contempt of court may be brought against anyone who makes, or causes to be made, a false statement in a document verified by a statement of truth." Signing a statement of truth is a serious legal act. A person who signs a statement of truth without an honest belief in its truth may be in contempt of court.

**Evidence-in-chief.** In family proceedings, the practice is for witness statements to stand as the witness's evidence-in-chief. This means the witness does not read the statement aloud at the hearing; the judge has already read it. The witness confirms the statement is true, and the other party then proceeds to cross-examine.

**Exhibits.** If the statement refers to documents — photographs, messages, medical records, school reports — those documents are attached as exhibits. Each exhibit is labelled with the witness's initials and a number (for example, "JB-1", "JB-2"). The statement refers to the exhibit by that label. Exhibits are part of the court bundle.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Stick to facts, not argument.** A witness statement contains evidence — things you have seen, heard, or experienced. It is not the place for legal submissions, conclusions about what the law requires, or character assessments of the other party. Factual, specific evidence (dates, what was said or done, the effect on the child) is what the judge needs. Opinions and argument belong, if anywhere, in closing submissions.

**Number every paragraph.** This is a requirement, not a convention. Numbered paragraphs allow the judge and the other party to refer precisely to what is being questioned during cross-examination. An unnumbered statement makes the hearing harder to conduct and may create an adverse impression.

**Label and attach exhibits properly.** Referring to a document in the statement without attaching it as a labelled exhibit means the court does not have the evidence you are referring to. Check that every document mentioned in the statement is attached, labelled, and included in the bundle.

**The statement of truth is a serious obligation.** Every witness statement includes a declaration that the contents are true. This is not a formality. If you include a fact you know to be false, or omit something material, you may face consequences in the proceedings — and in serious cases, contempt of court. Write only what you honestly believe to be accurate.

**Once filed, the other party can cross-examine on every part of it.** Write the statement with the knowledge that every sentence will be read by the other party and their solicitor or barrister, who will look for inconsistencies, omissions, and things they can challenge. The statement should be complete and honest — not just favourable. Gaps in a statement are as likely to be used against you in cross-examination as the things you chose to include.

## Key terms

**Statement of truth** — the declaration at the end of a witness statement confirming that the party honestly believes the facts stated are true; false statements made under a statement of truth may constitute contempt of court.

**Evidence-in-chief** — the evidence a party presents in support of their own case; in family proceedings, this is taken as read from the filed witness statement rather than given orally in examination-in-chief.

**Cross-examination** — questioning of a witness by the opposing party, directed at testing the accuracy and reliability of their evidence; a witness may be cross-examined on the contents of their witness statement at the hearing.

**Exhibit** — a document referred to in a witness statement and attached to it; exhibits are labelled with the witness's initials and a sequential number and form part of the court bundle.

**Hearsay** — a fact not within the witness's own direct knowledge that the witness has been told by a third party; hearsay is admissible in family proceedings but must be identified as such.
