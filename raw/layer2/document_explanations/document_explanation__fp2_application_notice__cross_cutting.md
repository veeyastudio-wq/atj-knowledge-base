# FP2 — how to apply for something within existing court proceedings

## What it is

Form FP2 is the application notice used to ask a court for something within family proceedings that have already started — or to go back to the court after a final order has been made. It is the form you use when you need to ask for something extra, make a change to an existing order, or request the court's permission to do something, without starting an entirely new case. It is a general-purpose form that applies across divorce, financial remedy, child arrangements, adoption, and other family proceedings.

## When it happens

FP2 is used at any point in live proceedings — for example, to apply for an interim order while waiting for a final hearing, to ask the court to vary or extend an order already made, to request more time, to seek disclosure of documents held by another party or a third party, or to make an urgent application without notice to the other side. It is also used after a case has ended, to go back to court to enforce or vary a final order — for example, if circumstances have changed or the other party has not complied.

It is not the form used to start a case from scratch. Starting proceedings uses different forms: C100 for child arrangements, D8 for divorce, Form A for financial remedy, FL401 for domestic abuse protection orders.

## What it involves

**The three parts of the form.** FP2 has three sections:
- **Part A** — who you are and what order you are asking the court to make. State clearly what you want the court to do and briefly why.
- **Part B** — tick boxes to confirm what evidence you are relying on (a witness statement, a statement in Part C itself, or both), and whether you want the court to deal with the application without a hearing.
- **Part C** — optional written evidence set out on the form itself, with its own statement of truth.

**Attach a draft order.** Where possible, attach a draft of the order you are asking the court to make. This is not mandatory, but courts expect it for anything beyond a simple procedural request. It helps the court understand precisely what you want and speeds up the process.

**Without a hearing.** FP2 includes a tick box asking the court to deal with the application without a hearing — that is, on paper. This is appropriate for straightforward procedural applications where the other party is unlikely to object, or where the matter is so urgent it cannot wait for a hearing to be listed. The court may reject the request and list a hearing instead if it considers one necessary.

**Without notice.** If the application is urgent and giving the other party advance notice would create a risk of harm or defeat the purpose of the application, you can apply without notice. A without-notice application should say why notice was not given. If the court grants the order without notice, it will usually list a further hearing to give the other party the chance to respond.

**Filing.** File the completed FP2 at the court dealing with the existing proceedings. A court fee is usually payable unless a fee remission applies. If your address should not be disclosed to the other party, do not put it on the form — file it separately on Form A65.

**What happens after.** The court will either list the application for a hearing and notify both parties, deal with it on paper and send out the outcome, or ask for further information before deciding. The other party is generally served with a copy of the application and any supporting evidence unless the court has ordered otherwise.

**FP2 across the tracks.** In financial remedy proceedings, FP2 is commonly used for interim maintenance applications, applications to freeze assets (a freezing order), or applications to extend time for filing documents. In child arrangements proceedings, it is used to vary or enforce an existing order, or to apply for permission where permission is needed — for example, to remove a child from the jurisdiction temporarily. In divorce proceedings, it is used for procedural applications such as extending time or amending directions.

**FP2 versus C2.** Form C2 is a separate form used specifically for applications in existing children proceedings — for example, for contact, specific issues, or to add a party. C2 is for children proceedings only. FP2 is the broader form used when no specific form exists for the application you need to make, or when the application is not covered by C2 or another dedicated form.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**FP2 is how you ask for anything you need mid-case that is not already scheduled.** If the court gave you a set of directions at your last hearing and you now need something different — more time, a new order, access to a document the other party is withholding — FP2 is usually the mechanism.

**Be specific about what you want.** Part A asks you to state the order you are seeking. Vague requests such as "I want the court to make things fair" are not enough. Identify the specific order — for example, "an order that the respondent provide copies of their last three tax returns by [date]" or "an order extending the time for filing my Form E by 21 days." Attach a draft if you can.

**Give reasons in plain English.** Part A also asks why you are applying. Set out the facts briefly — what has happened, why the order is needed, and why now. You do not need to cite law unless you are relying on a specific rule.

**Check whether you need permission first.** Some applications require the court's permission before the substantive application can be made. If you are unsure whether permission is needed, the court office can usually tell you, or this will be set out in the practice direction for the type of application you are making.

**Without-notice applications need a clear reason.** If you apply without notice and the court grants the order, the other party will be served and given the chance to come back to court. If your reason for not giving notice was weak, the court may be critical when it reviews the order at the return hearing. Be ready to explain why notice was not safe or appropriate.

## Key terms

**Application notice** — a formal request made to the court within existing proceedings; FP2 is the standard application notice form for family proceedings under Part 18 FPR.

**Part 18 FPR** — the part of the Family Procedure Rules 2010 that governs applications within proceedings, as distinct from the rules for starting proceedings.

**Without notice** — an application made without telling the other party in advance; also called ex parte; appropriate only where giving notice would defeat the purpose of the application or create a risk.

**Without a hearing** — a request that the court deal with the application on paper rather than listing it for a hearing; the court retains the discretion to list a hearing regardless.

**Interim order** — an order made during proceedings before the final hearing, intended to deal with a situation temporarily while the case continues.

**Draft order** — a written version of the order you are asking the court to make, attached to the FP2; it allows the judge to see exactly what is being requested and to approve or amend it.
