# Non-molestation order — applying for protection from abuse or harassment

## What it is

A non-molestation order is a court order that prohibits a person from molesting, harassing, pestering, or using or threatening violence against the applicant or any relevant child. It is a protective order made under Part IV of the Family Law Act 1996. Breaching a non-molestation order without reasonable excuse is a criminal offence.

## When it happens

A non-molestation order can be applied for at any point — before, during, or independently of other family proceedings. It does not require divorce proceedings or child arrangements proceedings to be underway. Applications can be made on notice (with the respondent informed in advance) or without notice in urgent cases where immediate protection is needed.

## What it involves

**The application.** The application is made using Form FL401, which sets out the applicant's relationship to the respondent, the behaviour the applicant is seeking protection from, and whether the application is being made with or without notice. A supporting statement setting out the specific incidents or pattern of behaviour — in particular any threats, acts of violence, harassment, or pestering — is required alongside the form.

**Without notice applications.** If the application is made without notice, the respondent is not told about the hearing in advance. If the court grants the order, it is served on the respondent and a further hearing is listed to give the respondent the opportunity to respond and to challenge the order if they wish. Without notice applications are appropriate only where giving prior notice would expose the applicant or a child to a risk of harm that justifies proceeding without the respondent being informed.

**Duration and renewal.** A non-molestation order is time-limited — typically up to 12 months — and can be renewed by application to the court. The order specifies the conduct it prohibits, which may be expressed in general terms, in relation to specific acts, or both.

**Breach.** Breach of a non-molestation order without reasonable excuse is a criminal offence under section 42A of the Family Law Act 1996. It is punishable by up to five years' imprisonment. Breach is a matter for the police and Crown Prosecution Service, not only a family court enforcement matter.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Legal aid is available.** Victims of domestic abuse applying for a non-molestation order can qualify for legal aid regardless of their financial means, subject to providing evidence of domestic abuse. This is one of the limited areas in family law where legal aid remains broadly accessible. The legal aid evidence requirements can be met in a number of ways — including a letter from a GP, police records, a referral from a MARAC, or other specified evidence. An application for legal aid should be made to a legal aid provider before the FL401 is filed, if time permits.

**Without notice is not automatic.** A without notice application is appropriate only where giving notice would expose the applicant or a child to risk of harm. The court will consider whether without notice was justified, and if the application could have been made on notice without exposing anyone to risk, the court may adjourn to give the respondent an opportunity to respond. Being able to explain specifically why prior notice was not safe is important.

**The supporting statement should be specific.** The statement accompanying the FL401 should describe specific incidents with dates where possible and explain the pattern of behaviour. Vague or general statements — "he has been abusive throughout the relationship" — are less likely to satisfy the court than statements that identify what happened, when, and what effect it had. Where there are police reports, hospital records, or other corroborating evidence, referring to them and attaching them as exhibits strengthens the application.

**A non-molestation order is separate from other proceedings.** Obtaining a non-molestation order does not automatically affect any child arrangements or financial proceedings. It does not determine who the children live with or what the financial settlement will be. It will, however, be relevant context in those proceedings and may affect how the court approaches safeguarding and risk considerations.

## Key terms

**Non-molestation order** — a protective court order under Part IV of the Family Law Act 1996 that prohibits a respondent from molesting, harassing, pestering, or using or threatening violence against the applicant or a relevant child.

**Molestation** — in family law, a broader concept than physical violence; it includes harassment, pestering, intimidation, and behaviour that interferes with the applicant's peace and comfort, as well as acts of physical violence and threats.

**Without notice application** — an application made without informing the respondent in advance; also called an ex parte application; appropriate only where giving notice would create a risk of harm.

**On notice application** — an application made with prior notice to the respondent, who has the opportunity to attend the hearing and respond before any order is made.

**FL401** — the application form for a non-molestation order or an occupation order under Part IV of the Family Law Act 1996.
