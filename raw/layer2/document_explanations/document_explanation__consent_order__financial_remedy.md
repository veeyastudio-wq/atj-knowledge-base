# Consent order — recording a financial agreement with the court

## What it is

A consent order is a court order that records the financial terms both parties have agreed and makes those terms legally binding and enforceable. An agreement between separating parties, however detailed and however sincerely reached, is not enforceable through the courts until it has been approved by a judge and sealed as a court order. The consent order is the mechanism by which a private agreement becomes a legal order.

## When it happens

A consent order can be submitted to the court at any point after both parties have reached full agreement on their financial terms — before proceedings are issued, during proceedings, or after. In practice, most consent orders arise in one of three situations: after settlement is reached through mediation without court proceedings having been issued; after settlement is reached at or following the FDR hearing; or as a negotiated settlement after the FDR that avoids a final hearing. A consent order must be approved by a judge even where both parties are in complete agreement. It is not self-executing.

## What it involves

**What the document contains.** The consent order sets out the complete terms of the financial agreement — what is happening to the family home, how any other property and assets are to be divided, whether any lump sum payments are to be made, pension arrangements, and any ongoing maintenance obligations. It also typically includes a clean break clause, which prevents either party from making future financial claims against the other once the order takes effect.

**The D81 statement of information.** Alongside the draft consent order, both parties must file a Form D81 — a statement of information about their current financial positions. The judge uses the D81 to assess whether the proposed terms are fair. The consent order will not be approved without a D81.

**How the court considers it.** In most cases, the judge considers the consent order and D81 on paper, without a hearing. The judge is not a rubber stamp: they must be satisfied that the terms are fair and within the range of outcomes a court could properly order. If the judge is not satisfied, they may request further information, invite the parties to attend a hearing, or decline to approve the order.

**What approval means.** Once approved and sealed by the court, the consent order is a court order in the full legal sense. Its terms are enforceable through the court's enforcement powers. A party who fails to comply with the terms of a consent order is in breach of a court order.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**A consent order is not automatically approved.** The fact that both parties agree does not mean the court will approve the order. A judge reviews the D81 and the draft order and must be independently satisfied that the terms are fair and proper. An order that appears to give one party significantly less than a court would award — without a good reason apparent from the papers — may be queried or rejected.

**Both parties must provide the D81.** The D81 is a summary of each party's current financial position. It is required even when both parties have already exchanged full Form E disclosure. Failing to file a D81 will delay approval.

**Once sealed, the consent order is very difficult to reopen.** A consent order can only be set aside in limited circumstances — for example, where it was obtained by non-disclosure of a material asset, by fraud, or where there has been a fundamental change in circumstances of a kind the order did not contemplate. The threshold for reopening a consent order is high. This is why getting the terms right before submission matters.

**Drafting the order correctly matters.** An ambiguous or poorly drafted consent order can be unenforceable or require a return to court to clarify what the parties actually agreed. The terms must be specific, complete, and legally precise. Standard consent order templates are available, and for straightforward cases they can be adapted by an unrepresented party — but complex matters involving pensions, businesses, or trust assets carry greater drafting risk.

## Key terms

**Consent order** — a court order recording the financial terms agreed between the parties; legally binding once approved and sealed by the court.

**D81** — a form setting out each party's current financial position, filed alongside the draft consent order to allow the judge to assess whether the proposed terms are fair.

**Clean break** — a clause in a consent order that prevents either party from making future financial claims against the other; once a clean break order is made, neither party can return to court to seek further financial provision from the former spouse.

**Sealed order** — a court order that has been formally approved and stamped by the court; only a sealed order is enforceable.
