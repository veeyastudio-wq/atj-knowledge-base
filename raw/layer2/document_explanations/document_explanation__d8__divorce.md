# Form D8 — the divorce application

## What it is

Form D8 is the official application form used to start divorce proceedings in England and Wales. Filing it formally begins the divorce process and triggers the court's timetable. The same form is used for the dissolution of a civil partnership and for judicial separation applications. Filing Form D8 starts the divorce track only — it does not start financial remedy proceedings or child arrangements proceedings, which are separate legal processes requiring their own applications.

## When it happens

Form D8 is filed at Stage 2 of the divorce track, after the decision to proceed with divorce has been made. The marriage must have lasted at least one year before an application can be made. There is no requirement to have resolved financial matters or child arrangements before filing, but the relationship between the divorce timetable and the financial remedy timetable — in particular, when the final order can be applied for — is important to understand before filing.

## What it involves

**What the form asks for.** Form D8 asks for the full names and addresses of both parties, details of the marriage (date, place, and the marriage certificate itself), the basis for jurisdiction, and whether the application is being made solely by one party or jointly by both.

**Sole versus joint application.** Under the no-fault divorce procedure introduced in 2022, neither party needs to make allegations against the other. A sole application is made by one party alone; a joint application is made by both. In a sole application, the respondent must be served with the application and file an acknowledgement of service (Form D10). In a joint application, both parties are applicants and there is no service requirement.

**What happens after filing.** The court issues the application and assigns a case number. In a sole application, the court serves the application on the respondent, who then has a period to file their acknowledgement. The 20-week cooling-off period begins from the date the application is issued — not from the date it is filed.

**The marriage certificate.** The original marriage certificate (or a certified copy) must be filed with the D8. A certified copy can be obtained from the General Register Office. If the certificate is in a foreign language, a certified translation is also required.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**The form is completed online or on paper.** The D8 can be submitted through the MyHMCTS online portal, which is the most common method, or filed on paper by post to the appropriate divorce centre. The online process guides the applicant through each question in sequence.

**Sole versus joint application — what the difference means in practice.** A joint application avoids the need to serve the form on the other party and removes the acknowledgement of service step, which simplifies the process where both parties are in agreement about proceeding. A sole application does not require the other party's consent — the divorce can proceed even if the respondent does not engage with the process (though delays may result).

**Form D8 starts the divorce track only.** Filing Form D8 does not start financial remedy proceedings or create any court process for resolving finances. If financial matters need to be resolved — and they almost always do — a separate Form A application must be made. Similarly, child arrangements proceedings require a separate C100 application. Many people filing a D8 do not yet know that three separate processes may need to run in parallel.

**The 20-week period begins on the issue date.** The court's issue date (when the application is formally processed by the court) is the date from which the 20-week cooling-off period runs, not the date the form is submitted or received.

## Key terms

**Sole application** — a divorce application made by one party (the applicant) without the other party's participation; the respondent is served with the application and must file an acknowledgement.

**Joint application** — a divorce application made by both parties together; removes the need for service and acknowledgement of service.

**No-fault divorce** — the divorce procedure under the Divorce, Dissolution and Separation Act 2020, in force since April 2022, under which neither party is required to make allegations against the other to obtain a divorce.

**Conditional order** — formerly called a decree nisi; the court's formal statement that the applicant is entitled to a divorce; made at Stage 4 of the divorce track. The marriage does not end at this stage.

**20-week cooling-off period** — the mandatory minimum period between the issue of the D8 application and the point at which a conditional order can be applied for.
