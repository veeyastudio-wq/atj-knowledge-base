# Position statement — setting out your case before a hearing

## What it is

A position statement is a short written document submitted to the court before a hearing that sets out a party's position on the issues the hearing will address. It is not a statement of evidence — it does not contain witness evidence or attempt to prove facts. Its purpose is to help the judge understand, before the hearing begins, what each party is seeking and what the key issues are.

## When it happens

Position statements are required or expected before most substantive hearings in both financial remedy and child arrangements proceedings: the FDA, FDR, FHDRA, DRA, and final hearings. The court directions order will usually specify the deadline for filing and serving the position statement — typically two to five days before the hearing. Even where a position statement is not formally directed, submitting one is standard practice and assists the judge.

## What it involves

**What a position statement typically covers:**

- The order or orders the party is seeking from the hearing
- The party's position on any issues in dispute that the hearing must address
- Any procedural matters the party wishes to raise — for example, a request for an adjournment, an objection to a document, or a point about the other party's compliance with directions
- In financial remedy cases, a brief summary of the party's current position on the financial settlement if the hearing is the FDR or final hearing

**Length and format.** A position statement is usually two to four pages. It should be written in plain, direct language, presented under clear headings, with numbered paragraphs. It is addressed to the court, not to the other party.

**What it is not.** A position statement is not a witness statement — it does not contain evidence. It is not a legal submission — it does not cite authority or argue points of law at length (that belongs in closing submissions, if relevant). It is not a letter of complaint about the other party's conduct. It sets out what is sought and why, concisely.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**The position statement is the judge's first view of your position on the day.** Most judges read position statements before the hearing begins. A clear, focused statement that identifies the issues and states what is sought helps the judge use the hearing time efficiently and means the hearing starts from an informed baseline.

**Write in plain, direct language.** The position statement does not need to use legal terminology. Clear, factual sentences are more effective than elaborate language. The judge is looking for what you want and the key reason you want it — not a comprehensive account of the history of the case.

**A position statement is not the place for evidence or argument.** Reproducing the contents of a witness statement, reciting a long history of events, or making extended legal arguments makes a position statement harder to use. The directions will specify separately if a witness statement is required; the position statement is a different document with a different purpose.

**File and serve it on time.** A late position statement — or no position statement — means the judge may open the hearing without context about your position. Opposing solicitors routinely file position statements; an unrepresented party who does not is at a practical disadvantage at the start of the hearing.

**Serve it on the other party at the same time as filing.** The other party should receive your position statement at the same time it is filed with the court — not after. Check the directions order for the exact deadline and method.

## Key terms

**Position statement** — a short pre-hearing document setting out a party's position on the issues the hearing will address; not evidence.

**Directions** — the judge's formal instructions, usually contained in a court order, specifying what each party must file and by when before the next hearing.
