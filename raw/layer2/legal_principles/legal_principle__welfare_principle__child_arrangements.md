# The welfare principle — the child's interests come first

## What it is

The welfare principle is the foundational rule of family law in England and Wales: when a court is making any decision about a child's upbringing, the child's welfare is the court's paramount consideration. Paramount means it takes priority over every other consideration — including the preferences, feelings, or perceived rights of either parent.

## Where it comes from

The welfare principle is established by section 1(1) of the Children Act 1989. It applies to every decision the family court makes about a child's upbringing, including decisions about with whom the child lives, when the child spends time with each parent, and any other significant question about the child's life. Section 1(3) of the same Act sets out the welfare checklist — the specific factors the court must work through when applying the principle in contested cases.

## What it means in practice

When a judge applies the welfare principle, they are working through the welfare checklist — a structured set of factors that the law requires them to consider. Those factors are:

- **The child's ascertainable wishes and feelings**, considered in light of the child's age and level of understanding. An older child's expressed views carry more weight than a younger child's; views are never determinative on their own, but they are always considered.
- **The child's physical and emotional needs.** This covers day-to-day care, stability, housing, education, and the importance of maintaining meaningful relationships with both parents where it is safe to do so.
- **The likely effect on the child of any change in circumstances.** The court examines the impact of both the status quo continuing and the proposed arrangements being implemented.
- **The child's age, sex, background, and any other characteristics the court considers relevant.** This includes cultural background, religious upbringing, and any specific needs arising from the child's individual circumstances.
- **Any harm the child has suffered or is at risk of suffering.** Harm includes physical harm, emotional harm, and exposure to domestic abuse between adults in the household.
- **How capable each parent (and any other relevant person) is of meeting the child's needs.** The court assesses each parent's capacity, not their comparative worthiness as a person.
- **The range of powers available to the court.** The judge considers what type of order — if any — is necessary and proportionate.

A judge does not score each factor and add them up. The checklist is a framework for structured analysis. The judge weighs the factors together, with the child's welfare as the single overriding objective.

The welfare principle applies at every hearing in the child arrangements track — not just the final hearing. Interim orders, directions about contact, and decisions about what evidence to obtain are all made with the child's welfare as the primary lens.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

**The court is not deciding who is the better parent.** The judge is asking one question: what outcome best serves this child's welfare? A parent who treats the proceedings as a contest to be won — or who approaches the hearing as an opportunity to present grievances about the other parent — is addressing the wrong question.

**The frame of the case must be the child's welfare, not parental entitlement.** Every statement, every document, and every submission in child arrangements proceedings carries more weight when it connects clearly to the welfare checklist. A position that explains why a particular arrangement serves the child's emotional development, stability, or safety is engaging with the language the judge is using. A position that focuses on what the parent deserves, what they have been denied, or what is fair to them is not.

**The child's wishes and feelings are one factor among several — not a veto.** A child expressing a preference to live with one parent or to stop spending time with the other is a factor the court must consider, but it does not determine the outcome. The weight given to the child's views depends on their age and understanding, and the court will also consider whether those views have been influenced by the circumstances of the proceedings.

**Raising welfare concerns requires specificity.** Where a party raises concerns about the other parent's capacity to meet the child's needs, or about a risk of harm, the court requires specific factual evidence — not general allegations or characterisations. Vague assertions of risk carry less weight than particularised accounts tied to the welfare checklist factors.
