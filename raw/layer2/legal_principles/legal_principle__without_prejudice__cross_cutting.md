# Without prejudice — protecting settlement communications from the court

## What it is

The without prejudice rule protects communications made in a genuine attempt to settle a dispute from being shown to the court as evidence. A letter, email, or conversation marked without prejudice — or understood to be part of settlement negotiations — cannot generally be referred to in court if the case does not settle. The purpose is to allow both parties to negotiate openly without fear that anything they offer or concede will be used against them if negotiations break down.

## Where it comes from

The without prejudice rule is a principle of common law, recognised and applied in courts in England and Wales. It does not derive from a statute. In family proceedings, it is given specific procedural expression in the Family Procedure Rules 2010 — most significantly in the rules governing the FDR hearing, which is conducted entirely on a without prejudice basis. The FDR provisions are in FPR Rule 9.17.

## What it means in practice

Communications must be part of a genuine attempt to settle for the rule to apply. Labelling a letter or email "without prejudice" does not automatically make it protected if it is not actually a settlement offer or negotiation. A letter that is simply asserting a position, threatening proceedings, or making complaints is not without prejudice communication merely because it carries that label. The substance of the communication determines whether the rule applies.

The distinction between without prejudice and open correspondence is important. Open correspondence can be shown to the court at any time — and often is, for example when a party wishes to demonstrate that a reasonable offer was made and rejected. Without prejudice correspondence cannot be shown to the court unless both parties agree. The choice of whether to write openly or without prejudice is a deliberate one.

The FDR hearing is a special procedural application of the without prejudice rule. Everything said at the FDR — including the judge's indication of the likely outcome — is without prejudice and cannot be referred to at the final hearing. This is why a different judge must hear the final hearing: the judge who gave an indication at the FDR is treated as if they have been told things that cannot be used at trial. This structure creates a space in which both parties and the judge can speak candidly about settlement without those communications being held against either party.

"Without prejudice save as to costs" (WPSATC) is a common variant used in financial remedy proceedings. Correspondence marked in this way cannot be shown to the court during the substantive hearing, but can be referred to after the judge has given judgment, when the court is deciding whether to make a costs order. It is used to demonstrate that a party made a reasonable settlement offer at an earlier stage — which, if the other party rejected it and obtained a worse outcome at trial, may lead to a costs order against the party who refused the offer.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

Marking correspondence without prejudice is a practical tool available to an unrepresented party. It allows a settlement offer or a willingness to explore compromise to be expressed in writing without that offer being treated as an admission if the case proceeds. An unrepresented party can write a without prejudice letter setting out what they are prepared to accept — and if the case does not settle and the offer is not accepted, that letter cannot be produced at the final hearing.

Receiving a without prejudice letter from the other party does not mean they are conceding anything or that an agreement has been reached. It means they are exploring whether settlement is possible. The fact that it is marked without prejudice means the discussions are confidential to both parties.

If the other party sends correspondence without the without prejudice label, it is open correspondence and can be referred to in court. This distinction matters when deciding how to respond to communications.

The FDR hearing is a genuine opportunity to settle. The frankness of the judge's indication at the FDR — which would not be given at a final hearing — is a direct consequence of the without prejudice protection. The judge can say what they think the outcome is likely to be precisely because that view cannot be reported outside the FDR. This is worth understanding before the hearing: the indication, however unwelcome, reflects a judicial view of how the case is likely to be decided.
