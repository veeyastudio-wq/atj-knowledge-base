# The duty of full and frank disclosure — what it means to tell the whole truth about your finances

## What it is

Both parties in financial remedy proceedings are under a legal duty to provide full, frank, and clear disclosure of their entire financial position. This is not a procedural formality — it is a substantive legal obligation that applies continuously from the start of proceedings until they conclude. Incomplete, inaccurate, or deliberately withheld disclosure is a serious matter with significant legal consequences.

## Where it comes from

The duty of full and frank disclosure is a well-established principle of financial remedy law, grounded in the requirement under the Family Procedure Rules 2010 that parties provide complete financial information to the court and to each other. The court's ability to exercise its powers under the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 depends on having an accurate picture of both parties' financial positions. Any order made on the basis of incomplete or misleading disclosure is potentially open to challenge.

## What it means in practice

The duty covers every aspect of a party's financial position, including:

- All bank and savings accounts, including accounts held abroad or in another person's name
- All property and real estate interests, wherever located
- All investments, shareholdings, and business interests — including minority shareholdings and interests in family businesses
- All pension entitlements, including deferred pensions and pensions held with former employers
- All income, including income from self-employment, rental income, and irregular income
- All liabilities and debts
- All financial resources that are or may become available, including anticipated inheritance, bonus payments, or the proceeds of a pending transaction

The duty continues beyond Form E. If a party's financial circumstances change materially after Form E is exchanged — a new job, the sale of a property, a significant inheritance, a business transaction — that change must be disclosed to the other party and to the court without waiting to be asked.

**The consequences of non-disclosure are significant.** A judge who discovers during the proceedings that a party has withheld or misrepresented their financial position can draw adverse inferences — treating the undisclosed asset as larger or more valuable than the evidence suggests, or treating the non-disclosing party's case with scepticism across all issues. A consent order that is later found to have been obtained through non-disclosure can be set aside by the court, even years after it was made, potentially requiring the parties to return to court and the financial settlement to be reconsidered from the beginning.

In the most serious cases, deliberate non-disclosure can give rise to contempt of court proceedings.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

**The duty applies equally whether or not a party has a solicitor.** An unrepresented party cannot later rely on a lack of legal knowledge as an explanation for incomplete disclosure. The obligation to disclose is imposed on the party personally, not on their legal representative.

**Form E is the primary mechanism for disclosure — but it is not the end of the obligation.** Completing Form E is how each party initially discharges the duty. But the duty continues after exchange. A change in financial circumstances between the exchange of Form E and the final hearing must be brought to the attention of the court and the other party. Waiting until a hearing to reveal a significant change — or hoping that it will not be noticed — is not compliant with the duty.

**Assets held in other people's names must be disclosed.** The fact that an asset is held in a company name, a family member's name, or a trust does not remove it from the disclosure obligation if the party has a beneficial interest in it or a reasonable expectation of benefiting from it. The test is substance, not legal form.

**Forgetting is not the same as withholding — but omissions still have consequences.** An unrepresented party who genuinely forgets to include an account or asset can correct the omission through updated disclosure, and courts distinguish between innocent omission and deliberate concealment. However, repeated or significant omissions — whether or not deliberate — affect the credibility of the party's disclosure and may affect how the judge treats the overall case.

**Questionnaires from the other party are part of the process, not an attack.** After Form E is exchanged, each party may raise a questionnaire — a list of requests for further documents or information. Responding to questionnaire requests promptly and completely is part of discharging the ongoing disclosure obligation. Refusing to answer or providing partial answers to legitimate requests delays the proceedings and may result in a court order compelling disclosure.
