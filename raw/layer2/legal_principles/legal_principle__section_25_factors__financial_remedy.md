# The section 25 factors — how a judge divides assets in a financial remedy case

## What it is

Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 sets out the factors a family court must take into account when deciding how to divide a couple's finances on divorce. There is no fixed formula — the judge weighs all the factors together against the overarching objective of reaching a fair outcome for both parties and for any children of the family.

## Where it comes from

Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 is the primary statutory provision governing financial remedy decisions. It has been in force, in broadly its current form, since 1984. Case law has developed a framework of three broad objectives — needs, sharing, and compensation — that courts use to structure the application of the section 25 factors, but these are tools of analysis, not separate legal rules.

## What it means in practice

When a judge is deciding a financial remedy case, they must consider:

- **The welfare of any minor children of the family.** This is the court's first consideration — it takes priority over all other section 25 factors. Housing and financial provision for children who live primarily with one parent will be central to any financial order.

- **The income, earning capacity, property, and other financial resources of each party**, both now and in the foreseeable future. This includes earning potential — not just current earnings. A judge will consider whether a party could reasonably be expected to increase their income through employment or retraining.

- **The financial needs, obligations, and responsibilities of each party**, now and in the foreseeable future. Housing needs are usually the most significant factor in practice, particularly where children are involved.

- **The standard of living enjoyed by the family during the marriage.** This does not mean the court tries to replicate that standard after separation — available resources often do not allow it — but it forms part of the picture.

- **The age of each party and the duration of the marriage.** A short marriage typically leads to a different outcome than a long one. An older party with limited earning years remaining has different needs from a younger one.

- **Any physical or mental disability of either party.** Disability affecting earning capacity or care needs is a relevant factor.

- **The contributions made by each party**, including contributions to the welfare of the family through home-making and child-rearing. The law treats non-financial contributions — caring for children, supporting a partner's career — as equivalent in principle to financial contributions.

- **The conduct of each party**, but only where it would be inequitable to disregard it. In practice, conduct is rarely a decisive factor. The threshold for conduct to affect the financial outcome is high; courts have consistently held that the breakdown of the marriage itself, and most behaviour associated with it, does not meet the threshold.

- **The value of any benefit either party will lose the chance of acquiring** as a result of the divorce. This most commonly arises in relation to pension entitlements — particularly the loss of a spouse's pension that would have been shared on the other spouse's death.

The judge applies these factors without a predetermined weighting. The result in each case turns on the specific facts. Needs — particularly housing needs arising from the care of children — often drive the outcome in cases where total assets are modest relative to those needs.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

**There is no automatic 50/50 split.** A common assumption is that the starting point is equal division and that the judge will then adjust from there. That is not an accurate description of how section 25 works. Equal division may be the right outcome in some cases — particularly long marriages where both parties have broadly equivalent needs and the assets are sufficient to meet them — but it is not the default position. The judge starts with the section 25 factors, not with a presumption of equality.

**Needs often outweigh the sharing principle.** Where the total assets are limited and one party — usually the one with primary care of the children — has an acute housing need, that need will typically take priority over a mathematical equal division. A party who enters proceedings insisting on 50/50 without engaging with the other party's needs is likely to have that position tested by the judge.

**Conduct is rarely relevant.** The financial remedy track is not the place to present an account of the other party's behaviour during the marriage. The judge is not there to adjudicate fault. Conduct only affects the outcome if it would be inequitable to disregard it — a threshold that most conduct, however distressing, does not meet. Time spent presenting conduct arguments that do not meet this threshold is time not spent on the factors that matter.

**Non-financial contributions count.** A party who left employment to care for children, or who supported the other party's career at the expense of their own, has made a contribution the law recognises as equivalent in principle to direct financial contributions. This is particularly relevant in long marriages where there is a significant disparity in the parties' current earning positions.

**Pension must be considered.** Pension entitlements built up during the marriage are a matrimonial asset. A party who does not raise pension in proceedings — or who agrees a financial settlement without addressing pension — may be giving up a significant entitlement. Pension sharing orders and pension attachment orders are available to the court.
