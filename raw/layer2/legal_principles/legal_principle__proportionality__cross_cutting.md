# Proportionality — the court's duty to deal with cases justly

## What it is

The proportionality principle requires the court to deal with a case in a way that is proportionate to its nature, importance, and complexity. It sits within the overriding objective of the Family Procedure Rules 2010 — the foundational rule that governs how courts are required to manage all family proceedings. A court applying proportionality will limit the time, cost, and procedural steps in a case to what is genuinely necessary to resolve the issues fairly.

## Where it comes from

Family Procedure Rules 2010, Rule 1.1 — the overriding objective. Rule 1.1 requires the court to deal with cases justly, having regard to any welfare issues involved. Dealing with a case justly includes dealing with it in ways that are proportionate to the nature, importance, and complexity of the issues. The duty to further the overriding objective applies to the court in managing proceedings and to the parties in assisting the court.

## What it means in practice

The court actively manages cases with proportionality in mind. Judges can and do limit the evidence that will be heard, restrict the length of hearings, refuse permission for expert evidence that is unnecessary given what is actually in dispute, and decline to hear arguments they consider disproportionate to the issues. These powers exist precisely because court resources are limited and because the cost of litigation — to both parties and to the court — should not exceed what the dispute warrants.

Proportionality applies to both parties. A party with greater resources cannot run an unlimited litigation strategy simply because they can afford to do so. The court's active case management function is partly a counterweight to that kind of imbalance.

The principle shapes what the court will and will not allow at each hearing. At a procedural hearing such as the FDA or FHDRA, the court's focus is on what is needed to progress the case — not on hearing everything each party wants to say at that stage. Attempts to introduce extensive evidence, raise multiple peripheral issues, or extend the length of an interlocutory hearing beyond what the agenda requires will generally be curtailed.

Conduct that is disproportionate — generating excessive correspondence, raising irrelevant issues, refusing reasonable requests, or pursuing satellite disputes — can be reflected in costs orders. Courts have a wide discretion in costs and the conduct of the parties is a relevant factor.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

Understanding proportionality helps when deciding what to raise at each hearing and how to frame it. Courts expect submissions to be focused and relevant. Raising too many issues at once, or issues that are peripheral to the core dispute, can reduce the time available for the matters that actually need to be decided — and may create an adverse impression.

The practical question at each stage is not "what is everything I want the court to know about?" but "what does this hearing need to decide, and what evidence or argument is necessary for that?" Knowing the purpose of each hearing — what a FHDRA is for, what an FDA is for — is part of being able to use court time effectively.

If the other side has more money than you and appears to be using it against you — burying you in paperwork, sending lengthy letters about minor issues, making applications that serve no real purpose, or generally trying to wear you down financially — proportionality is the principle you can invoke. You can ask the judge directly to manage the case more actively: to limit correspondence, put a stop to unnecessary applications, or make clear that the court will not allow litigation to be used as a financial weapon. Reference the overriding objective and FPR Rule 1.1.

If a judge cut you off at a hearing, stopped you mid-submission, or said they did not need to hear everything you wanted to say, that is proportionality in action — not a sign that the judge is against you. Courts are required to keep hearings focused on what that hearing is actually for. At a first hearing or directions appointment, the judge's job is to set a timetable and give directions, not to hear the full story from both sides. Being stopped from going through everything you prepared does not mean your case has been prejudged; it means the court is managing its time proportionately. You will have the opportunity to say what needs to be said at the appropriate stage.
