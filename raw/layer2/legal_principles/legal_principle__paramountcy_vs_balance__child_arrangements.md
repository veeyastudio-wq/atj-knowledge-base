# Parental rights versus the child's welfare — why the court's focus is not on you

## What it is

The family court in child arrangements proceedings is not adjudicating between two competing parental rights. Parents in England and Wales do not hold rights against each other or against the court in relation to their children — they hold parental responsibility, which is a set of duties and powers exercised for the benefit of the child. The court's question at every stage is not which parent has the stronger claim, but what outcome best serves the welfare of this child.

## Where it comes from

Parental responsibility is defined in section 3 of the Children Act 1989 as the rights, duties, powers, responsibilities, and authority which a parent has in relation to a child — but the legislation and case law consistently interpret these as instruments for meeting the child's needs, not as entitlements parents hold for their own benefit. The welfare principle in section 1(1) of the Children Act 1989 — that the child's welfare is the court's paramount consideration — gives effect to this orientation throughout the proceedings.

## What it means in practice

In practice, this means that when a judge is assessing what order to make, they are not conducting a comparative assessment of the two parents as people. They are not asking who is the more deserving parent, who has been treated more fairly by the other party, or whose account of events is more credible in a general sense. They are asking a narrower and more specific question: given everything the court knows about this child and these circumstances, what arrangements will best serve this child's welfare?

A parent's perspective — their parenting capacity, their relationship with the child, their proposed arrangements, and any concerns they raise about the other parent — is relevant because and insofar as it bears on that question. Evidence of the other parent's failings is relevant where it points to a risk to the child's welfare; it is less relevant where it amounts to an account of how the parent has been treated in the relationship.

This does not mean that a parent's experience, distress, or genuine concerns are dismissed. It means that for those concerns to carry weight with the court, they need to be framed around their effect on the child.

## What it means if you're unrepresented

**The language of rights does not translate in the family court.** Statements such as "I have a right to see my child," "I am being denied my parental rights," or "it is unfair that I cannot see my child when I have done nothing wrong" are expressed in the language of entitlement. The family court speaks a different language — one centred on the child's welfare. A position expressed as "regular, meaningful contact with me serves my child's emotional development and sense of identity" communicates the same underlying concern in the language the court is designed to respond to.

**Parental responsibility is not affected by the outcome of proceedings — in most cases.** Both parents with parental responsibility retain it regardless of what child arrangements order is made. An order determining where a child lives or how much time they spend with each parent does not remove or diminish parental responsibility. A parent who conflates the two — believing that losing a residence dispute means losing parental status — has a fundamental misconception that may affect how they approach the hearing.

**The court is not there to correct perceived unfairness between the parents.** If one parent behaved badly during the relationship — was unfaithful, was controlling, was unsupportive — the family court is not the mechanism by which that behaviour is recognised or sanctioned, unless it translates into a direct risk to the child. The court will not make a child arrangements order that punishes a parent for their conduct toward the other adult, or that rewards a parent for being the wronged party.

**Framing submissions around the child changes how they land with the judge.** A submission that explains, specifically, why a particular arrangement serves the child's stability, relationships, emotional needs, or safety — referring where possible to the welfare checklist factors — is addressing the question the judge is trying to answer. The same underlying concern, expressed as a grievance about the other parent or as a claim to personal entitlement, is not.

**CAFCASS and the judge are both oriented to the child.** Neither the judge nor the CAFCASS officer is there to validate either party's experience of the relationship breakdown. Both are focused on the child. Engaging with that focus, rather than competing against it, is more effective at every stage of the proceedings.
