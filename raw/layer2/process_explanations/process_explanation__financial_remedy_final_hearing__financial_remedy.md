# What happens at a financial remedy final hearing?

## What it is

The financial remedy final hearing is the hearing at which a judge makes a binding financial order if the parties have been unable to reach a settlement at the Financial Dispute Resolution hearing (FDR) or at any point beforehand. Both parties present their case, give oral evidence, and may cross-examine each other. The judge then applies the statutory framework for financial remedy decisions to reach a fair outcome and makes an order that is binding on both parties.

## When it happens

The final hearing is Stage 6 of the financial remedy track. It takes place only if the case has not settled at the FDR (Stage 5) or through subsequent negotiation. The date is set by the judge at or after the FDR. The final hearing judge must be a different judge from the one who conducted the FDR. Final hearings in financial remedy cases can last from half a day to several days, depending on the complexity of the assets involved.

## What it involves

**Before the hearing**, each party must file and serve a bundle of documents in the format specified by the court, a statement of issues, an updated open proposals document, a costs schedule (Form H1), and in some cases updated valuations or expert reports. The court will give specific directions about filing deadlines at the FDR or in subsequent directions.

**At the hearing**, the typical sequence is:

1. **Opening submissions.** Each party summarises their case and what order they are asking the court to make. This is usually brief.

2. **Evidence.** Each party gives oral evidence — they present their witness statement as their evidence-in-chief (usually taken as read) and are then cross-examined by the other party. Any witnesses who have filed statements may also give evidence and be cross-examined.

3. **Closing submissions.** Each party makes submissions on the law and the facts, addressing the statutory factors the judge must consider and arguing why the order they seek is appropriate. This is often the most technically demanding stage for an unrepresented party.

4. **Judgment.** The judge may give judgment at the end of the hearing (an ex tempore judgment) or may reserve judgment to be handed down in writing at a later date.

5. **The order.** Once judgment is given, the terms of the financial order are finalised, either agreed between the parties or settled by the judge. A sealed copy of the order is then issued by the court.

**The judge's decision** is reached by applying the statutory framework for financial remedy — a set of factors the court is required to consider. These factors include the parties' respective financial resources and needs, the length of the marriage, the standard of living enjoyed during the marriage, and the welfare of any children. This framework is addressed in a separate entry.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**This is the most demanding hearing for an unrepresented party.** Cross-examination, legal submissions, evidence handling, and understanding what the judge is weighing against what are all tasks that a barrister would normally perform. An unrepresented party is expected to conduct each of these stages themselves.

**Cross-examination requires preparation.** Cross-examining the other party effectively means identifying the weakest points in their evidence and probing them with focused questions. Emotional confrontation or long speeches are not effective and may be interrupted by the judge. Questions must relate to facts that are in dispute and relevant to the court's decision.

**Closing submissions require engagement with the law.** The judge expects to be addressed on the statutory factors, the applicable case law, and why the outcome sought is consistent with those. An unrepresented party is not held to the same standard as a barrister, but a submission that ignores the legal framework entirely carries less weight.

**The bundle must be prepared correctly.** The court bundle — the paginated set of documents provided to the judge — must comply with the court's directions. A bundle that is incomplete, incorrectly paginated, or missing required documents creates practical difficulties at the hearing and may attract adverse comment.

**Settlement remains possible up to judgment.** The case can settle at any point before the judge delivers the final judgment, including on the morning of the hearing, during a break, or during the hearing itself. If agreement is reached, the terms must be put to the judge immediately and reduced to a consent order.

**Costs.** At the end of a final hearing the judge may make a costs order against a party who has behaved unreasonably in the proceedings. Filing a costs schedule (Form H1) is a procedural requirement; an unrepresented party who has not done so may be at a disadvantage if costs are argued.

## Key terms

**Final hearing** — the hearing at which the judge makes a binding financial order after hearing evidence from both parties.

**Oral evidence** — evidence given by a witness in person at a hearing, as opposed to a written statement alone; at the final hearing each party gives oral evidence and is subject to cross-examination.

**Cross-examination** — questioning of a witness by the opposing party, designed to test the accuracy and reliability of their evidence.

**Closing submissions** — each party's argument to the judge at the end of the hearing, addressing the evidence and the law and explaining why the order they seek is appropriate.

**Ex tempore judgment** — a judgment delivered verbally by the judge immediately after hearing submissions, rather than reserved in writing.

**Reserved judgment** — a judgment that the judge does not deliver immediately but hands down in writing at a later date.

**Statutory framework** — the set of legal factors prescribed by statute (in financial remedy cases, primarily the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973) that the judge must consider when making a financial order.

**Form H1** — a statement of costs filed by each party before a financial remedy final hearing, setting out the costs they have incurred in the proceedings.

**Consent order** — a court order recording agreed financial terms; if the parties reach agreement during the final hearing, a consent order can be submitted to the judge in place of a contested judgment.

**Court bundle** — the paginated collection of documents — statements, valuations, correspondence, and court orders — provided to the judge for use at the hearing.
