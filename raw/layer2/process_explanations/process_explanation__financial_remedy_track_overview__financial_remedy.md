# The financial remedy track — an overview of all six stages

## What it is

The financial remedy track is the legal process by which a separating couple's finances — property, savings, pensions, income, and debts — are divided, either by an agreement approved by the court or by a court order after a hearing. It is entirely separate from the divorce track and runs on its own timetable. Without a court-sealed financial order, neither party's financial claims against the other are closed, even after the divorce final order is made.

## When it happens

The financial remedy track is triggered by separation, not by the filing of a divorce application. It can begin before or after the divorce application is issued. It normally runs concurrently with the divorce track's 20-week cooling-off period (Stage 3 of the divorce) and should be resolved — at minimum to a sealed consent order — before the divorce final order is applied for. Applying for the divorce final order while financial matters remain open can affect the ability to make certain financial claims, including claims against a former spouse's pension or estate. Most people going through separation encounter the divorce and financial remedy tracks at the same time, and not recognising that they are two distinct legal processes is a common source of confusion and procedural error.

## What it involves

The financial remedy track has six stages:

**Stage 1 — Pre-proceedings (MIAM)**
Before filing a court application, both parties are required to attend a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting (MIAM) with an accredited family mediator, unless a specific exemption applies. The MIAM explores whether mediation or another form of dispute resolution is suitable. If both parties reach agreement in mediation, that agreement can be made legally binding by submitting a draft consent order to the court — if the court approves it, no further stages are needed.

**Stage 2 — Filing Form A**
If court proceedings are necessary, the applicant files Form A — a notice of intention to proceed with an application for a financial order. The court issues a timetable and fixes a date for the First Appointment. The respondent is notified.

**Stage 3 — Financial disclosure (Form E)**
Both parties must complete Form E — a detailed financial statement covering all assets, income, liabilities, pensions, business interests, and outgoings — and exchange it simultaneously with the other party on the date set by the court. This is the most technically demanding stage for unrepresented parties: the form is long, the supporting documentation requirements are extensive, and incomplete or inaccurate disclosure has consequences throughout the remainder of proceedings.

**Stage 4 — First Appointment (FDA)**
The court holds a short directions hearing. The judge's primary role at this stage is to identify the issues in genuine dispute, ensure that disclosure is complete, and set a timetable toward the FDR hearing. The FDA is not a negotiation hearing; neither party is expected to make or respond to settlement offers.

**Stage 5 — Financial Dispute Resolution (FDR)**
The FDR is a without-prejudice hearing at which a judge gives an indication — not a binding ruling — of the likely outcome if the case were to proceed to a final hearing. The parties are expected to negotiate on the day, with a view to reaching a settlement. Many financial remedy cases resolve at or shortly after the FDR stage.

**Stage 6 — Final hearing**
If the FDR does not result in settlement, the case proceeds to a final hearing before a different judge. Both parties give oral evidence and are subject to cross-examination. The judge makes a binding financial order.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Financial remedy is a separate track from the divorce.** The divorce process does not automatically deal with finances. Financial claims against a former spouse remain open until a court order is made — potentially for many years after the marriage ends.

**The MIAM is a legal requirement, not an optional step.** Attending a MIAM (or establishing and evidencing a valid exemption) is a prerequisite to filing Form A. Domestic abuse, urgency, and certain other specified circumstances can provide an exemption.

**Form E is the most demanding stage for unrepresented parties.** The form requires full, frank, and up-to-date disclosure of every aspect of both parties' financial positions. Errors, omissions, or late disclosure can result in adjournments, costs penalties, or — in serious cases — contempt of court proceedings. Preparing the supporting documents — bank statements going back twelve months, pension cash equivalent transfer values (CETVs), mortgage statements, property valuations — takes time and should be started well in advance of the exchange date.

**The FDR judge cannot make a final order.** The FDR is a settlement hearing. What is said and offered there is without prejudice and cannot be referred to at the final hearing. The judge who presides at the FDR is disqualified from presiding at the final hearing.

**Resolve finances before applying for the divorce final order.** Applying for the divorce final order before a financial order is in place removes certain court powers and can affect rights to a former spouse's pension, estate, and other financial entitlements.

## Key terms

**Financial order** — a court order that determines how a couple's finances are divided. Types include property adjustment orders, lump sum orders, pension sharing orders, and periodical payments orders.

**Consent order** — a financial order made by the court to record an agreement reached between the parties. It must be submitted to and approved by a judge to become legally binding.

**Form A** — the form used to notify the court of an intention to apply for a financial order, which starts court proceedings.

**Form E** — the financial statement that each party must complete in full and simultaneously exchange, disclosing all assets, income, liabilities, and pensions.

**MIAM** — Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting; the required pre-proceedings step.

**CETV** — Cash Equivalent Transfer Value; the value assigned to a pension for the purposes of financial remedy proceedings.

**Without prejudice** — a label applied to statements or offers made during negotiation, preventing them from being referred to in court proceedings; it allows parties to negotiate freely without their offers being used against them.

**FDA** — First Appointment; the initial directions hearing in financial remedy proceedings.

**FDR** — Financial Dispute Resolution hearing; the without-prejudice, settlement-focused hearing at which a judge indicates the likely outcome.
