# The child arrangements track — an overview of all six stages

## What it is

The child arrangements track is the legal process by which a court determines with whom a child lives, how much time the child spends with each parent, and — through related order types — other significant decisions about the child's upbringing, when the parents or carers cannot reach agreement. It is a separate track from the divorce and the financial remedy process, with its own forms, timetable, and court proceedings. At the centre of every decision the court makes is the welfare principle: the child's welfare is the court's paramount consideration, above the wishes or rights of either parent.

## When it happens

The child arrangements track can begin at any point — before, during, or after divorce proceedings. It is not triggered by a divorce application and is not dependent on financial remedy proceedings being underway or concluded. It runs on its own timetable. Where all three tracks are running simultaneously, each proceeds independently according to its own hearings and deadlines. The child arrangements track may also arise entirely independently of divorce, between parents who were never married.

## What it involves

The child arrangements track has six stages:

**Stage 1 — Pre-proceedings**
Before making a court application, a parent is required to attend a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting (MIAM) with an accredited mediator, unless a specific exemption applies. Exemptions include cases involving domestic abuse, child protection concerns, urgency, and certain other specified circumstances. If parents reach an agreed parenting arrangement through mediation or direct negotiation, no court application is needed; the agreement may be formalised as a consent order if the parties wish.

**Stage 2 — Filing the application (C100 form)**
If court proceedings are necessary, the applicant files Form C100 with the court. Where there are allegations of harm or domestic abuse involving the child or either party, Form C1A is filed alongside it. The court serves the application on the respondent and notifies CAFCASS. The court fee is payable on filing (with exemptions available based on financial circumstances).

**Stage 3 — Cafcass safeguarding checks**
On receipt of the application, CAFCASS conducts safeguarding checks with police and children's services in respect of all parties. Before the first hearing, a CAFCASS officer contacts both parents by telephone. The officer then prepares a safeguarding letter for the court, setting out any identified welfare concerns or safeguarding risks. The safeguarding letter is an initial risk assessment, not a full welfare report.

**Stage 4 — First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment (FHDRA)**
The FHDRA is usually listed within four to six weeks of the application being issued. A CAFCASS officer attends. The hearing has two primary objectives: to explore whether agreement can be reached on the day or with further dispute resolution support, and — if not — to give directions for how the case will proceed. Many cases settle at or shortly after the FHDRA.

**Stage 5 — Dispute Resolution Appointment (DRA)**
If the case has not settled after the FHDRA, a DRA is listed. By this stage, the court may have ordered a full CAFCASS welfare report (a Section 7 report), which will have been filed before the DRA. The DRA is a further opportunity for settlement before the expense and formality of a final hearing. The judge may also give final directions if a final hearing is unavoidable.

**Stage 6 — Final hearing**
At the final hearing, both parties give oral evidence and may be cross-examined. Where a Section 7 report has been prepared, the CAFCASS officer who wrote it may also give evidence. The judge applies the welfare principle and the welfare checklist, and makes a child arrangements order that is legally binding on both parties.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**The welfare principle governs every decision.** The court's paramount consideration is the welfare of the child — not the rights, feelings, or preferences of either parent. The judge will apply the welfare checklist from section 1(3) of the Children Act 1989, which includes factors such as the child's ascertainable wishes and feelings (considered in light of age and understanding), the child's physical and emotional needs, the likely effect of any change in circumstances, and any risk of harm.

**CAFCASS is an independent investigator, not a support service for either party.** CAFCASS officers investigate both parties and report their findings to the court. The safeguarding letter and any Section 7 report they prepare directly influence the judge's decisions. Both parents are contacted by CAFCASS before the first hearing, and both should treat those conversations as part of the formal process.

**The FHDRA is not a final hearing.** The first hearing is primarily about case management and exploring whether settlement is possible. Final orders are rarely made at the FHDRA unless both parties agree in full.

**A Section 7 report takes time.** Where the court orders a full welfare report, preparation typically takes several months. The report sets out the CAFCASS officer's findings on each parent's circumstances, the child's wishes and feelings, any welfare concerns, and a recommendation on arrangements. A Section 7 report is not automatically ordered in every case — the court decides whether one is necessary.

**The MIAM exemption for domestic abuse.** Where there is a history of domestic abuse involving a party or a child, attendance at a joint MIAM is not required. The exemption must be evidenced on the C100 form in the prescribed way. The court will scrutinise the basis for the exemption claimed.

## Key terms

**Child arrangements order** — a court order that sets out with whom a child lives and/or when the child spends time with a parent or other person. It replaces the former concepts of "residence order" and "contact order."

**CAFCASS** — Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service; an independent public body whose officers advise the family court on the welfare of children in proceedings. In Wales, the equivalent body is CAFCASS Cymru.

**Welfare principle** — the rule in section 1(1) of the Children Act 1989 that the child's welfare is the court's paramount consideration in any decision about the child's upbringing.

**Welfare checklist** — the list of specific factors the court must consider when applying the welfare principle, set out in section 1(3) of the Children Act 1989.

**Section 7 report** — a welfare report prepared by a CAFCASS officer (or in some cases a local authority) when ordered by the court; it sets out findings on the child's circumstances and makes recommendations about arrangements that would best serve the child's welfare.

**Safeguarding letter** — the initial document prepared by CAFCASS before the FHDRA, setting out the results of safeguarding checks and any identified risks. It is not the same as a Section 7 report.

**MIAM** — Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting; the required pre-proceedings step before a court application can be filed.

**C100** — the application form used to apply for a child arrangements order, a prohibited steps order, or a specific issue order under the Children Act 1989.

**C1A** — a supplemental form filed alongside C100 where there are allegations of harm or domestic abuse involving the applicant, respondent, or a child.

**FHDRA** — First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment; the initial hearing in private law child arrangements proceedings.

**DRA** — Dispute Resolution Appointment; a subsequent hearing at which settlement is explored before any final hearing.

**Prohibited steps order** — a court order preventing a parent from taking a specified step in relation to a child (for example, removing the child from the jurisdiction) without the court's permission.

**Specific issue order** — a court order giving directions about a specific question relating to parental responsibility, such as which school a child attends or whether a child receives a particular medical treatment.
