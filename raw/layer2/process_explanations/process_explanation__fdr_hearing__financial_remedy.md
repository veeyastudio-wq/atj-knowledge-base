# What is the Financial Dispute Resolution hearing (FDR)?

## What it is

The Financial Dispute Resolution hearing (FDR) is a judge-led settlement hearing in financial remedy proceedings. The judge hears brief summaries of both parties' positions and gives an indication of what a fair outcome might look like — this indication is not a binding judgment. The parties then negotiate, with the judge available to assist if needed. Most financial remedy cases that reach this stage settle at the FDR. The entire hearing is conducted on a without-prejudice basis: nothing said or offered at the FDR can be referred to at a final hearing if the case does not settle.

## When it happens

The FDR is Stage 5 of the financial remedy track. It takes place after the FDA (Stage 4), once any outstanding disclosure ordered at the FDA has been completed. The court sets the FDR date at the FDA. If the case settles at the FDR, no final hearing (Stage 6) takes place. If it does not settle, the FDR judge is disqualified from presiding at any subsequent final hearing, and the case is listed before a different judge.

## What it involves

Before the FDR, each party must file and serve on the other a statement of open proposals — a document setting out the financial settlement they are proposing. Both parties should also file an updated summary of their financial positions if anything has changed since Form E.

At the FDR hearing itself:

1. **Judicial indication.** The judge reads the papers before the hearing. Each party (or their advocate) gives a short summary of their position. The judge then gives an indication of how the case might be decided at a final hearing — what outcome the judge considers broadly fair, based on the evidence and the applicable legal framework. This indication is not a judgment and does not bind anyone.

2. **Negotiation.** The parties then negotiate — often outside the courtroom, in the courthouse corridors or in separate rooms. The judge remains available throughout the day to hear further submissions or give further indications if the parties reach an impasse on a specific point.

3. **Settlement or adjournment.** If the parties reach agreement, a consent order is drafted and submitted to the judge for approval that day or shortly after. If no agreement is reached by the end of the hearing, the judge gives directions for a final hearing and the case is listed before a new judge.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**The judicial indication is not a decision.** The judge's assessment of a likely outcome at the FDR is an indication only. It carries significant weight — it is what an experienced judge expects to happen if the case goes to a final hearing — but it is not an order. The parties remain free to negotiate around it.

**"Without prejudice" protects both parties.** Everything said and every offer made at the FDR is without prejudice. This means neither party can refer to what was said, offered, or indicated at the FDR if the case proceeds to a final hearing. The purpose is to allow both parties to negotiate freely without fear that an offer will be used against them. Understanding this protection before arriving is important — it allows frank discussion.

**The FDR judge cannot hear the final hearing.** If the case does not settle at the FDR, the judge who gave the indication is disqualified from conducting any subsequent final hearing. This is to ensure that the final hearing judge comes to the evidence without knowledge of any without-prejudice offers or discussions.

**Arriving without settlement proposals is not an option.** Each party is required to file open proposals before the FDR. A party who attends without having engaged with settlement — with no proposals and no response to the other party's proposals — will be at a significant disadvantage and may face adverse comment from the judge.

**Negotiation happens in the building, not just in the courtroom.** Much of the FDR takes place in the corridors and waiting rooms of the courthouse. Parties without legal representatives negotiate directly with each other or through submissions back to the judge. Knowing this in advance — and understanding that this negotiation is normal and expected — helps avoid surprise.

**A consent order must be drawn up if agreement is reached.** If the parties settle, the terms must be reduced to a draft consent order before the judge can approve them. An unrepresented party who has not prepared a draft order or does not know how to draft one may need to seek urgent assistance on the day.

## Key terms

**FDR** — Financial Dispute Resolution hearing; the without-prejudice, judge-led settlement hearing in financial remedy proceedings.

**Without prejudice** — a principle that prevents statements or offers made during settlement negotiations from being used as evidence in court proceedings. Everything at the FDR is without prejudice.

**Judicial indication** — the judge's assessment, given at the FDR, of what outcome would be broadly fair if the case were decided at a final hearing. It is not binding on anyone.

**Open proposals** — written settlement proposals that are not without-prejudice and that can be referred to at a final hearing; each party must file these before the FDR.

**Consent order** — a court order recording the terms of a financial settlement agreed between the parties; it must be approved by a judge to become legally binding.

**Form E** — the detailed financial statement each party completes before the FDA, setting out all assets, income, liabilities, and pensions.

**FDA** — First Appointment; the procedural directions hearing that precedes the FDR.
