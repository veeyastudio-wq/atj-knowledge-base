# What is the First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment (FHDRA)?

## What it is

The First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment (FHDRA) is the initial court hearing in a private law child arrangements case. Its dual purpose is to explore whether the parties can reach an agreement on the day — with the assistance of a CAFCASS officer and potentially the judge — and, if not, to give directions for how the case will proceed. The FHDRA is not a final hearing. The full merits of each party's case are not argued here.

## When it happens

The FHDRA is Stage 4 of the child arrangements track. It takes place after the application has been issued (Stage 2) and after CAFCASS has conducted its safeguarding checks and prepared a safeguarding letter (Stage 3). The FHDRA is usually listed within four to six weeks of the application being issued. Before the FHDRA can take place, CAFCASS must have completed its safeguarding enquiries and the safeguarding letter must have been filed with the court.

## What it involves

**Before the FHDRA**, CAFCASS contacts both parties by telephone. CAFCASS checks police records and children's services records for each party and uses the telephone conversations to identify any safeguarding concerns. The findings are set out in the safeguarding letter, which is filed with the court before the hearing. Both parties receive a copy.

**A CAFCASS officer attends the FHDRA** and is available to speak with both parties separately before the hearing begins. This is a normal and expected part of the process — CAFCASS is present to assist the court and, where it is safe and appropriate, to facilitate discussion between the parties.

**At the FHDRA**, the judge considers:

- The CAFCASS safeguarding letter and any identified welfare or safeguarding concerns.
- Whether the case can be resolved by agreement at this hearing, either with or without the judge's direct involvement.
- If the case cannot be resolved, what evidence or reports will be needed and what directions must be given.

**If agreement is reached** on the day, the judge may make a consent order setting out the agreed arrangements. This brings the proceedings to an end without the need for any further hearings.

**If the case does not settle**, the judge gives directions. These may include:

- An order for a full welfare report (a Section 7 report) from CAFCASS.
- Directions for the filing of witness statements.
- Referral to a local authority for a Section 37 report if there are public law concerns.
- A further hearing — usually a Dispute Resolution Appointment (DRA) — with a date set.
- In some cases, an interim child arrangements order governing contact or residence while the proceedings continue.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Read the safeguarding letter before the hearing.** CAFCASS prepares the safeguarding letter based on police and children's services checks and on the telephone conversations with both parties. Both parties should receive it before the hearing date. The letter may contain factual errors or characterisations of the telephone conversation that a party wishes to address. The FHDRA is the appropriate time to raise any concerns about the letter's accuracy — not through argument, but by calmly and specifically identifying what is said to be incorrect.

**CAFCASS is not an advocate for either party.** The CAFCASS officer at the FHDRA is an officer of the court. Their role is to assist the court in identifying welfare concerns and to facilitate discussion if it is safe to do so. Questions can be directed to the CAFCASS officer about what the safeguarding letter says and what evidence was relied upon.

**The FHDRA is not the time to present the full case.** The judge is not making a final decision at this hearing. Lengthy statements of grievance, detailed accounts of past events, or attempts to persuade the judge of the substantive position are not appropriate at the FHDRA and are unlikely to advance the case.

**Agreement reached at the FHDRA is binding.** If the parties reach an agreement and the judge makes a consent order, that order is legally enforceable. Agreeing to terms at the FHDRA without fully understanding what is being agreed — or without considering whether the terms are workable — can create problems later.

**An interim order may be made.** The judge may make an interim order governing arrangements for the child while the case continues. This is not the final decision, but it will govern the position until the next hearing.

**Position statements are useful but not always required.** While the FHDRA is not a full evidential hearing, a short written position statement setting out a party's concerns and what order they are seeking helps the judge understand the issues quickly. Some courts provide guidance on whether one is expected.

## Key terms

**FHDRA** — First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment; the first court hearing in private law child arrangements proceedings.

**CAFCASS** — Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service; the independent public body whose officers advise the family court on the welfare of children in proceedings.

**Safeguarding letter** — the document prepared by CAFCASS before the FHDRA, setting out the results of police and children's services checks and any identified safeguarding concerns. It is not a full welfare report.

**Section 7 report** — a full welfare report prepared by a CAFCASS officer (or local authority) when ordered by the court; sets out findings on the child's welfare and makes recommendations about arrangements.

**Section 37 report** — a report ordered from the local authority where the court has concerns that a child may be suffering or at risk of suffering significant harm; used where public law concerns arise in private law proceedings.

**Consent order** — a court order recording the terms of an agreement reached between the parties; legally binding once sealed by the court.

**Interim order** — a temporary court order made during ongoing proceedings to govern the position until a final order is made.

**Directions** — the judge's formal instructions setting out what each party must do and by when before the next hearing.

**DRA** — Dispute Resolution Appointment; the hearing that typically follows the FHDRA if the case has not settled.
