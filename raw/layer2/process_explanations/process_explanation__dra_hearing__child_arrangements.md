# What is the Dispute Resolution Appointment (DRA)?

## What it is

The Dispute Resolution Appointment (DRA) is a hearing held after the FHDRA in child arrangements proceedings, once any evidence or reports ordered at the FHDRA have been filed. Its purpose is to review the evidence that has been gathered, explore whether the parties can now reach agreement, and — if agreement is still not possible — give final directions for a contested final hearing. Where a Section 7 welfare report has been ordered, it will be central to this hearing.

## When it happens

The DRA is Stage 5 of the child arrangements track. It takes place after the FHDRA (Stage 4) and after any directed evidence — typically a CAFCASS Section 7 report, and sometimes witness statements from each party — has been filed with the court. The DRA date is set by the judge at the FHDRA. If the case settles at the DRA, no final hearing (Stage 6) is needed. If it does not, the judge gives directions for a final hearing.

## What it involves

**Before the DRA**, any reports or statements directed at the FHDRA must be filed and served. Where a Section 7 report has been ordered, CAFCASS (or in some cases the local authority) prepares a full welfare report covering the child's circumstances, the child's wishes and feelings, each parent's situation, any welfare concerns, and a recommendation on what arrangements would best serve the child's welfare. Both parties receive the Section 7 report before the DRA.

**At the DRA**, the judge:

- Reviews the Section 7 report (if one has been ordered) and any other evidence that has been filed.
- Considers whether agreement is now possible in light of the evidence, including CAFCASS's recommendations.
- May facilitate or encourage settlement discussions between the parties.
- If agreement is reached, makes a consent order recording the agreed arrangements.
- If agreement is not reached, gives directions for a final hearing — setting a timetable for the filing of final witness statements, any updating evidence, and listing dates.

**The CAFCASS officer who wrote the Section 7 report** may attend the DRA. Either party may ask the court at the DRA for permission to question the CAFCASS officer about their report at the final hearing; this is called permission to cross-examine the officer.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Read the Section 7 report carefully before the DRA.** The report will contain the CAFCASS officer's findings on each party's parenting capacity and circumstances, the child's expressed wishes and feelings, any welfare concerns, and a recommendation. This recommendation carries significant weight with the judge. Understanding exactly what it says — including anything that is unfavourable — before the DRA is essential.

**Disagreeing with the Section 7 recommendation is possible, but requires clear reasoning grounded in the child's welfare.** A judge will take the CAFCASS recommendation seriously, but it is not binding. A party who disagrees with the recommendation needs to be able to articulate — specifically and calmly — why the recommended arrangements would not best serve the child's welfare, referring to the welfare checklist factors. A response that focuses on the party's own feelings or rights as a parent, rather than on the child's welfare, is unlikely to be persuasive.

**Factual errors in the Section 7 report can be challenged.** If the report contains a factual inaccuracy — a wrong date, a misquotation of what was said during the CAFCASS interview, or a mischaracterisation of circumstances — this can be raised at the DRA and, if necessary, preserved for cross-examination of the CAFCASS officer at the final hearing. Challenging the report effectively requires identifying specific, factual errors rather than expressing general disagreement with the officer's conclusions.

**Settlement at the DRA avoids the cost and stress of a final hearing.** If both parties can agree to arrangements in light of the Section 7 report and any other evidence, a consent order can be made at the DRA. The DRA is a genuine opportunity for resolution even in cases that have been contested since the FHDRA.

**Interim arrangements may have been in place since the FHDRA.** Any interim order made at the FHDRA governs the position during proceedings. If circumstances have changed materially since that interim order was made, the DRA is the appropriate time to raise this with the court.

**A position statement before the DRA helps the judge.** A short written document setting out the party's position in light of the evidence — what order they are seeking and why, engaging with the Section 7 report where relevant — assists the court to use the DRA hearing time efficiently.

## Key terms

**DRA** — Dispute Resolution Appointment; the hearing that follows the FHDRA once directed evidence has been gathered, aimed at settlement or final directions.

**Section 7 report** — a full welfare report prepared by a CAFCASS officer (or local authority) when ordered by the court; covers the child's circumstances, wishes and feelings, each parent's situation, and makes a recommendation about arrangements.

**CAFCASS** — Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service; the independent public body whose officers advise the family court on the welfare of children in proceedings.

**Welfare checklist** — the list of factors the court must consider when applying the welfare principle, set out in section 1(3) of the Children Act 1989; includes the child's wishes and feelings, their physical and emotional needs, the likely effect of any change in circumstances, and any risk of harm.

**Consent order** — a court order recording the terms of an agreement reached between the parties; legally binding once sealed by the court.

**Cross-examination** — questioning of a witness by the opposing party at a hearing, to test the accuracy and reliability of their evidence; a party who wishes to cross-examine the CAFCASS officer at the final hearing must obtain the court's permission.

**FHDRA** — First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment; the first hearing in child arrangements proceedings, which precedes the DRA.

**Interim order** — a temporary court order governing arrangements for the child during the proceedings, until a final order is made.

**Position statement** — a short written document filed before a hearing, setting out a party's current position, the order they are seeking, and the key points they wish to raise.
