# The divorce track — an overview of all five stages

## What it is

The divorce track is the legal process by which a marriage is formally ended in England and Wales. It follows a fixed sequence of five stages and, at its simplest, requires no court attendance. The divorce track deals only with ending the marriage itself — it does not resolve finances or arrangements for children, which are separate legal processes that typically run at the same time.

## When it happens

The divorce track begins when one or both spouses decide to end the marriage. It can be started at any time after the first anniversary of the marriage. It runs simultaneously with — but independently of — the financial remedy track and the child arrangements track. Most people going through family separation encounter all three tracks at once, and not recognising that they are three distinct legal processes is one of the most common sources of confusion at the outset of proceedings.

## What it involves

The divorce track has five stages:

**Stage 1 — Decision and first steps**
The applicant (or joint applicants) decides whether to apply alone or jointly, locates the original marriage certificate, considers whether the marriage was formed abroad, and decides whether financial matters and child arrangements need to be addressed in parallel proceedings.

**Stage 2 — Filing the application**
Form D8 is submitted to the court, either online through the MyHMCTS portal or by post, together with the court fee and the marriage certificate. In a sole application, the respondent receives the application and files a formal acknowledgement using Form D10.

**Stage 3 — The 20-week cooling-off period**
A mandatory minimum of 20 weeks runs from the date the application is issued. No application for a conditional order can be made before this period ends. This period is the normal time within which financial matters should be negotiated or resolved.

**Stage 4 — Applying for the conditional order**
Once 20 weeks have passed and at least one day after the applicant confirms their intention to proceed, the applicant applies for the conditional order using Form D84. The court considers the application on the papers; no hearing is usually required. The conditional order is the court's formal statement that the applicant is entitled to a divorce, but the marriage is not yet ended at this point.

**Stage 5 — The 6-week wait and final order**
At least six weeks after the conditional order is pronounced, the applicant may apply for the final order using Form D36. The final order is the order that legally ends the marriage. Applying for the final order before financial matters are resolved carries significant legal and financial risks (see below).

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Three tracks run simultaneously.** The divorce track, the financial remedy track, and the child arrangements track are three separate legal processes. Each has its own forms, timetables, and — where agreement cannot be reached — its own court proceedings. Completing the divorce does not resolve finances or child arrangements.

**Financial remedy should be resolved before the final order.** Financial remedy proceedings — the process of dividing assets, property, and pensions — should be concluded, at minimum to a sealed consent order, before the final order in divorce is applied for. Applying for the final order while financial matters remain open can affect the ability to make certain financial claims, including claims against a former spouse's pension or estate. The 20-week cooling-off period in Stage 3 exists partly to allow time for financial negotiations.

**The conditional order does not end the marriage.** The conditional order (Stage 4) is the court's confirmation that the divorce can proceed. The parties remain legally married until the final order (Stage 5) is made.

**Joint or sole application.** Either spouse may apply, or both may apply jointly. A joint application removes the need to serve the application on the other party and removes the acknowledgement of service step.

**No fault is required.** Since April 2022, divorce in England and Wales is no fault. Neither party needs to allege conduct or assign blame.

## Key terms

**Applicant** — the person who files the divorce application. In a joint application, both spouses are applicants.

**Respondent** — in a sole application, the spouse who receives the application and must file an acknowledgement of service.

**Conditional order** — formerly called a decree nisi; the court's formal statement that it is satisfied the applicant is entitled to a divorce. The marriage is not ended at this stage.

**Final order** — formerly called a decree absolute; the order that legally ends the marriage.

**Form D8** — the application form for a divorce, dissolution, or judicial separation order.

**Form D10** — the acknowledgement of service form completed by the respondent in a sole divorce application.

**Form D84** — the application for a conditional order.

**Form D36** — the application for a final order.

**MyHMCTS** — the HMCTS online portal through which divorce applications can be filed and managed.
