# What happens at the final hearing in child arrangements proceedings

## What it is

The final hearing is the hearing at which a judge makes a binding child arrangements order, having heard evidence from both parties and — where a welfare report was ordered — from the CAFCASS officer who prepared it. It is a contested hearing: both parties present their case fully, and the judge decides what arrangements best serve the child's welfare. The order made at the final hearing is legally binding on both parties.

## When it happens

The final hearing is Stage 6 of the child arrangements track. It takes place only if the case has not settled at the FHDRA (Stage 4), the DRA (Stage 5), or through any intervening negotiation. The hearing date is set by the judge at the DRA or in subsequent directions. By the time of the final hearing, any CAFCASS Section 7 report and any directed witness statements will already have been filed and served on both parties.

## What it involves

**Before the hearing**, each party files and serves their final witness statement — the written account of the evidence they want the judge to consider — together with any updating evidence directed by the court. A court bundle is also prepared: a paginated set of all the documents before the court, including the CAFCASS report, the statements, and any relevant orders or correspondence.

**At the hearing**, the typical sequence is:

1. **The applicant presents their case.** The applicant's witness statement stands as their evidence-in-chief. The respondent may cross-examine the applicant on their statement. The judge may also ask questions.

2. **The respondent presents their case.** The same process applies in reverse. The applicant may cross-examine the respondent.

3. **CAFCASS evidence (where a Section 7 report was ordered).** The CAFCASS officer who prepared the welfare report may give evidence. Both parties may ask questions of the officer if the court has given permission for cross-examination.

4. **Closing submissions.** Each party summarises their case and addresses the judge on what order they are seeking and why, with reference to the welfare checklist.

5. **Judgment.** The judge delivers a decision either at the end of the hearing or in writing at a later date. The judgment will explain how the welfare checklist has been applied to reach the decision.

6. **The order.** A child arrangements order is drawn up and sealed by the court. Both parties receive a copy.

**What the judge is deciding.** The judge's task is to apply the welfare principle — the child's welfare is the paramount consideration — by working through the welfare checklist. The judge considers the child's wishes and feelings (in light of their age and understanding), the child's physical and emotional needs, the likely effect of any change in circumstances, any risk of harm, and the capacity of each parent to meet the child's needs. The judge is not deciding which parent has been treated more fairly; the decision is focused entirely on what arrangements best serve the child.

## What to know if you're unrepresented

**Address the judge correctly.** In the family court, judges sitting as circuit judges or recorders are addressed as "Your Honour." District judges are addressed as "Sir" or "Ma'am." If uncertain, "Sir" or "Ma'am" is always acceptable.

**The hearing has a structure — understand it before you arrive.** The applicant presents first; the respondent second. Each party's written statement stands as their primary evidence, and the other party has the opportunity to challenge it through questions. Understanding this sequence means knowing when to speak, when to listen, and what the judge is doing at each stage.

**Cross-examination is focused questioning, not argument.** The purpose of questioning the other party is to test the accuracy of their evidence — to identify where it is incomplete, inconsistent, or contradicted by other evidence the court has. Long speeches, expressions of grievance, or attempts to persuade the judge of your general position during cross-examination are not effective and may be interrupted.

**The CAFCASS recommendation carries significant weight but is not binding.** If a Section 7 report has been prepared, its recommendations will be a central point of reference for the judge. A party who disagrees with the recommendation needs to engage with the welfare checklist specifically — explaining which factors point toward a different conclusion — rather than simply asserting a different preference.

**The order is binding and enforceable.** Once made, a child arrangements order is a legal obligation. Non-compliance with its terms — by either party — is a matter the court takes seriously. Understanding what the order says and what it requires is important before leaving the hearing.

## Key terms

**Child arrangements order** — a court order that sets out with whom a child lives and when the child spends time with each parent or other person. It replaces the former terms "residence order" and "contact order."

**Welfare checklist** — the list of factors set out in section 1(3) of the Children Act 1989 that a court must consider when making decisions about a child's upbringing; includes the child's wishes and feelings, physical and emotional needs, risk of harm, and the capacity of each parent to meet the child's needs.

**CAFCASS** — Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service; the independent public body whose officers advise the family court on the welfare of children in private law proceedings.

**Section 7 report** — a welfare report prepared by a CAFCASS officer (or local authority) when ordered by the court; sets out findings on the child's welfare and makes recommendations about arrangements.

**Evidence-in-chief** — the evidence a party presents in their own favour; in family proceedings this is usually taken as read from the filed witness statement, rather than given orally.

**Cross-examination** — questioning of a witness by the opposing party, directed at testing the accuracy and reliability of their evidence.

**Closing submissions** — each party's argument to the judge at the end of the hearing, explaining what order is sought and why the evidence and the welfare checklist support that outcome.
